# Build Conversion Sales Letter

## What This Skill Does

Takes an offer and architects the complete long-form sales letter — the single most revenue-dense asset a business can create. This skill sequences every section using Perry Belcher's 21-Step Sales Letter Formula, calibrates the opening strategy using Eugene Schwartz's 5 Levels of Market Awareness, and weaves Cialdini's 7 Principles of Persuasion throughout. The output is a structured blueprint that a copywriter (or the user) can execute with confidence.

The core principle: poor copy in the right sequence outperforms brilliant copy in the wrong sequence. Most sales letters fail not because the writing is bad but because the architecture is broken — they ask for the sale before building value, reveal price before anchoring, or open with features when the reader doesn't even know they have a problem. This skill fixes the architecture first.

This is not finished copy. It is the blueprint — the structural skeleton with key phrases, psychological annotations, and section-by-section direction. The user or a copywriter writes the final draft from this architecture. Think of it as the architect's drawings before the builders show up.

## Phase 1: Discovery Questions

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Don't just repeat it. Help them think through it — suggest options based on what they've already told you, offer examples from common patterns, or reframe the question. The goal is a real answer, not a placeholder.

**If the user has output from build-irresistible-offer, extract-testimonial-stories, or generate-persona-playbook:** Reference that output directly. Don't re-ask what you already know. Skip or condense questions where the answer already exists.

**If the user truly can't answer one** (e.g., no proof exists yet for a new offer), note it as a gap and keep moving. Flag it in Section 7 of the blueprint.

### The 8 Questions

**Q1: "What offer are you writing this sales letter for?"**
The product, service, program, or course. What it is, what it costs, and what the buyer gets. If they have output from build-irresistible-offer, pull the offer details from there and confirm.

*If stuck, ask:* "What are you selling, what does it cost, and what does the buyer get? Even a rough version is fine — we'll sharpen it."

**Q2: "Who is the reader? What's their awareness level?"**
Not just demographics — where do they sit on Schwartz's awareness spectrum? This determines the entire opening strategy.
- **Completely Unaware** — They don't know they have a problem
- **Problem Aware** — They know the problem, not the solution
- **Solution Aware** — They know solutions exist, haven't found yours
- **Product Aware** — They know your product, haven't bought yet
- **Most Aware** — They know you, trust you, just need the nudge

*If stuck, ask:* "Does the reader know they have a problem? Do they know solutions exist? Do they know YOUR solution exists? Have they already seen your offer before?"

**Q3: "What's the dream outcome — what does their life look like after?"**
Not features. The transformation. The vivid, specific, emotionally resonant picture of what changes.

*If stuck, ask:* "If I interviewed your best customer 6 months after buying, what would they say changed? What are they doing differently? How do they feel?"

**Q4: "What's the ONE big idea or mechanism that makes your solution different?"**
Every great sales letter has a unique mechanism — the reason YOUR approach works when others haven't. This is not a feature list. It's the single insight or method that makes the reader think "that's why nothing else worked."

*If stuck, ask:* "What's different about HOW you do this compared to everyone else? What did you figure out that the market hasn't caught on to yet?"

**Q5: "What proof do you have?"**
Testimonials, case studies, credentials, data, before/after results, media mentions, certifications, your own story. List everything, even if it feels small. If extract-testimonial-stories output exists, reference those directly.

*If stuck, ask:* "What evidence exists that this works? Even your own results count. What numbers, stories, or credentials can you point to?"

**Q6: "What are the top 5 objections someone would have before buying?"**
The real reasons people say no. Price, skepticism, time commitment, past failures, trust, alternatives, "I can do this myself," "this won't work for my situation."

*If stuck, ask:* "What do people say when they decide NOT to buy? What excuses do they make? What do they say they need to 'think about'?"

**Q7: "What's the guarantee?"**
The risk reversal mechanism. If they have output from build-irresistible-offer, pull the guarantee structure from there. If no guarantee exists, flag it — a missing guarantee is a conversion killer.

*If stuck, ask:* "If someone buys and it doesn't work, what happens? Refund? Extended service? Something else? What would make a skeptical buyer feel safe?"

**Q8: "What's the urgency — why should they buy NOW vs. later?"**
Real urgency only. What gets worse for them with every passing week? Is there a deadline, limited capacity, price increase, or closing enrollment? Manufactured scarcity destroys trust.

*If stuck, ask:* "What gets worse for them every week they wait? Is there a genuine deadline, limited spots, or price increase coming?"

## Phase 2: Generate the Sales Letter Blueprint

After gathering all 8 answers, generate a structured document with these 7 sections. Use the user's answers as raw material — synthesize, sharpen, and fill gaps. Don't parrot their words back.

### Section 1: Awareness Level Strategy

Based on Q2, determine the opening strategy. The awareness level dictates everything about how the letter begins and how much work each section must do.

| Awareness Level | Opening Strategy | Lead Type | Approximate Opening Length |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Completely Unaware | Pattern interrupt with a dramatic story that surfaces the problem indirectly | Story lead — narrative that pulls them in before they realize it's about their problem | 500-800 words before mentioning the product |
| Problem Aware | Agitate the problem they already feel, then bridge to your mechanism | Problem-agitation lead — "If you're struggling with [X], here's what's actually going on..." | 300-500 words of agitation before solution |
| Solution Aware | Differentiate your mechanism from alternatives they've already tried | Mechanism lead — "You've tried [alternatives]. Here's why they failed and what works instead." | 200-400 words differentiating before presenting |
| Product Aware | Lead with proof and a compelling incentive to act now | Proof lead — "[Name] achieved [result] in [time]. Here's the offer..." | 100-200 words of proof before the offer |
| Most Aware | Skip the buildup, make the offer directly with a reminder | Direct offer lead — "You've seen what this does. Here's why today is the day." | 50-100 words, straight to CTA |

Specify the exact strategy for this letter, including:
- Which awareness level the reader is at (from Q2)
- The opening approach and why it fits
- How much "warming up" the reader needs before the product is mentioned
- The emotional state to target in the opening (fear, curiosity, hope, frustration, aspiration)

### Section 2: The 21-Step Architecture

Organize the 21 steps into 5 blocks. For each step, provide:
- **Step purpose** — the one job this section does
- **Content direction** — what to write, drawing directly from the user's answers
- **Psychological principle** — which persuasion lever is at work (Cialdini, PAS, BAB, Hormozi, etc.)
- **Approximate word count** — how long this section should be
- **Key phrases or angles** — specific language, hooks, or structural templates to follow

#### BLOCK 1: HOOK (Steps 1-3) — Capture Attention, Make the Big Promise

**Step 1: Call Out to Your Audience**
- Purpose: Filter the right reader in and everyone else out
- Content direction: [Specific callout based on Q2 audience, using their language]
- Psychological principle: Identity — people pay attention when they feel seen
- Word count: 25-50 words
- Template: "Attention [specific identity]: If you [specific situation], then this is the most important [letter/page/message] you'll read this [year/quarter/month]."

**Step 2: Big Promise Headline**
- Purpose: State the dream outcome in one line that stops the scroll
- Content direction: [Based on Q3 dream outcome + Q4 mechanism]
- Psychological principle: Specificity creates believability
- Word count: 10-25 words
- Template: "How to [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe] without [specific pain point]"

**Step 3: Sub-headline Backing Up the Big Promise**
- Purpose: Add proof or credibility to the headline so it doesn't feel like hype
- Content direction: [Based on Q5 proof — a specific result, number, or credential]
- Psychological principle: Authority (Cialdini) — immediate credibility anchor
- Word count: 15-40 words
- Template: "[Proof element] that has already helped [number] [people/businesses] [achieve specific result]"

#### BLOCK 2: STORY (Steps 4-6) — Build Emotional Connection

**Step 4: Identify the Problem Using Storytelling**
- Purpose: Make the reader feel understood — show you know their situation intimately
- Content direction: [PAS framework — state the problem using the language from Q6 objections and Q3 current state]
- Psychological principle: Liking (Cialdini) — empathy creates trust. PAS — agitate before solving.
- Word count: 200-500 words
- Structure: Open with a relatable scenario → describe the symptoms they experience → name the hidden cause they haven't identified

**Step 5: Provide the Solution and Show the Transformation**
- Purpose: Bridge from pain to possibility — introduce the mechanism (not the product yet)
- Content direction: [BAB framework — Before state (from Q4) → After state (from Q3) → Bridge is the mechanism (from Q4)]
- Psychological principle: Commitment (Cialdini) — once they agree the mechanism makes sense, they're primed to want the product
- Word count: 200-400 words
- Structure: "What if there were a way to..." → introduce the big idea/mechanism → paint the after picture

**Step 6: Show Pain with Empathy and Affinity**
- Purpose: Demonstrate you've been where they are — build the "us vs. them" bond
- Content direction: [Origin story or founder story that mirrors the reader's struggle, leading to the discovery of the mechanism]
- Psychological principle: Unity (Cialdini) — shared identity. Liking — similarity builds trust.
- Word count: 300-600 words
- Structure: "I used to [same struggle] → I tried [same failed solutions] → Then I discovered [mechanism] → Everything changed"

#### BLOCK 3: PROOF (Steps 7-9) — Demonstrate Credibility and Results

**Step 7: Bullet Points of Benefits**
- Purpose: Rapid-fire value communication — bullets are the hardest-working element of any sales letter
- Content direction: [See Section 4 — Bullet Point Arsenal. Pull from Q3 dream outcome and Q5 proof]
- Psychological principle: Specificity + curiosity — each bullet creates a micro-desire
- Word count: 150-300 words (15-20 bullets)
- Format: Each bullet is one line, starts with a benefit or curiosity hook, ends with a specific outcome

**Step 8: Social Proof / Testimonials**
- Purpose: Let other people make the case — third-party validation is more persuasive than self-promotion
- Content direction: [From Q5 proof. If extract-testimonial-stories output exists, use those formatted testimonials. Arrange strongest first.]
- Psychological principle: Social Proof (Cialdini) — "people like me have done this and it worked"
- Word count: 200-500 words (3-5 testimonials)
- Format: Name + context + specific result + emotional statement. Before/after format preferred.

**Step 9: Build Authority / Credentials**
- Purpose: Establish why YOU are the person to deliver this — credentials, experience, track record
- Content direction: [From Q5 proof — credentials, media, certifications, years of experience, number of clients served]
- Psychological principle: Authority (Cialdini) — expertise signals reduce skepticism
- Word count: 100-250 words
- Format: Brief, factual, not boastful. "Over the past [X years], I've [specific accomplishment]..."

#### BLOCK 4: OFFER (Steps 10-15) — Present the Offer with Value Stacking

**Step 10: Make Your Offer**
- Purpose: State clearly and simply what they get — the first time the product/service is formally presented
- Content direction: [From Q1 offer details. Name the offer, describe what's included, frame it as the vehicle for the transformation from Q3]
- Psychological principle: Clarity — confusion kills conversions. One clear offer, not a menu.
- Word count: 100-200 words
- Format: "Here's what you get when you [join/enroll/buy]:" followed by the core deliverables

**Step 11: Add Bonuses**
- Purpose: Increase perceived value without increasing price — bonuses tip the scales
- Content direction: [From Q1 if bonuses exist. Each bonus should solve a secondary problem or accelerate results]
- Psychological principle: Reciprocity (Cialdini) — giving more than expected. Hormozi Value Equation — increase dream outcome and speed.
- Word count: 150-300 words
- Format: Each bonus gets a name, a value anchor, and a one-sentence description of what it solves

**Step 12: Build Up the Value**
- Purpose: Anchor the total value HIGH before revealing the price — the gap between value and price is the deal
- Content direction: [See Section 5 — Value Stack. Sum all components with individual values.]
- Psychological principle: Anchoring — the first number frames every number after it. Hormozi Value Equation — maximize perceived value.
- Word count: 100-200 words
- Format: Progressive stacking — list each component with its value, running total, then grand total

**Step 13: Reveal the Price**
- Purpose: Price reveal AFTER the value stack makes the price feel small by comparison
- Content direction: [From Q1. Frame as investment, not cost. Compare to alternatives, cost of inaction, or price per day/month]
- Psychological principle: Contrast — price feels small against the anchored value. Reframing — "not $X, but $Y"
- Word count: 50-150 words
- Format: "The total value is [$value stack total]. But you won't pay anywhere near that. Your investment today is just [$price]."

**Step 14: Inject Scarcity**
- Purpose: Create genuine reason to act now — without scarcity, "I'll do it later" wins
- Content direction: [From Q8 urgency. Only use REAL scarcity — limited spots, enrollment window, price increase, bonus expiration]
- Psychological principle: Scarcity (Cialdini) — perceived loss of availability increases desire
- Word count: 50-150 words
- Format: State the specific constraint, the reason it exists, and what happens when it's gone

**Step 15: Give the Guarantee (Risk Reversal)**
- Purpose: Remove the last barrier — make saying yes feel completely safe
- Content direction: [See Section 6 — Guarantee Copy. From Q7.]
- Psychological principle: Risk reversal — when the seller bears the risk, the buyer's decision becomes easy
- Word count: 100-250 words
- Format: Guarantee box — visually distinct section with clear terms, timeframe, and process

#### BLOCK 5: CLOSE (Steps 16-21) — Drive Action

**Step 16: Call to Action**
- Purpose: Tell them exactly what to do next — one clear, specific action
- Content direction: [Single CTA button/link with action-oriented language. "Join Now," "Enroll Today," "Get Instant Access"]
- Psychological principle: Commitment (Cialdini) — make the next step small and clear
- Word count: 25-75 words
- Format: Direct instruction → button/link → brief reassurance ("You'll get instant access to everything")

**Step 17: Give a Warning (What Happens If They Don't Act)**
- Purpose: Future-pace the PAIN of inaction — what does their life look like if nothing changes?
- Content direction: [Reverse of Q3 — if the dream outcome is the after, this is the "still stuck in the before" scenario]
- Psychological principle: Loss aversion — people are more motivated to avoid pain than to gain pleasure
- Word count: 100-200 words
- Format: "Here's what happens if you do nothing..." → describe the cost of staying where they are

**Step 18: Close with Reminder**
- Purpose: Summarize the key points — headline promise, mechanism, proof, offer, guarantee
- Content direction: [Condensed version of the strongest elements from the letter]
- Psychological principle: Recency effect — people remember what they read last
- Word count: 100-200 words
- Format: Brief recap hitting the 3-4 strongest selling points

**Step 19: P.S. (Restate Offer and Urgency)**
- Purpose: The P.S. is the second most-read element after the headline — treat it as a standalone pitch
- Content direction: [Restate the big promise + the best proof point + the urgency/scarcity + the CTA]
- Psychological principle: Curiosity + scarcity — many readers scroll to the P.S. first
- Word count: 50-150 words
- Format: "P.S. — [Restate the single most compelling reason to buy]. [Add urgency]. [CTA]."

**Step 20: Testimonials Repeated / Additional Proof**
- Purpose: Final proof layer for skeptics who scrolled to the bottom looking for validation
- Content direction: [2-3 additional testimonials or the strongest testimonial repeated. Different voices than Step 8 if possible.]
- Psychological principle: Social Proof (Cialdini) — reinforcement through repetition and variety
- Word count: 150-300 words
- Format: Result-focused testimonials — specific numbers, timeframes, and emotional outcomes

**Step 21: Final Close / Second CTA**
- Purpose: Last chance to convert — clean, simple, no friction
- Content direction: [Same CTA as Step 16, possibly with a final one-line reminder of the guarantee or urgency]
- Psychological principle: Commitment (Cialdini) — after reading this far, they're invested. Make it easy to say yes.
- Word count: 25-50 words
- Format: One sentence + CTA button/link

### Section 3: Headline Options

Provide 5 headline options using different angles. Each headline includes a sub-headline and a brief note on why it works for this specific audience and awareness level.

**Option 1: Benefit Headline** — Lead with the dream outcome
- Headline: [Specific outcome + timeframe + without pain point]
- Sub-headline: [Proof element or credibility anchor]
- Why it works: [Connection to audience's primary desire]

**Option 2: Story Headline** — Lead with the narrative hook
- Headline: [Dramatic opening of the founder/client story]
- Sub-headline: [Bridge to relevance — why this story matters to the reader]
- Why it works: [Connection to awareness level — especially effective for Unaware/Problem Aware]

**Option 3: Proof Headline** — Lead with a specific result
- Headline: [Client name/type + specific measurable result + timeframe]
- Sub-headline: [How they did it — introduces the mechanism]
- Why it works: [Specificity creates believability — especially effective for Solution Aware/Product Aware]

**Option 4: Question Headline** — Challenge a belief
- Headline: [Question that challenges a common assumption the reader holds]
- Sub-headline: [Hint at the answer — introduces curiosity gap]
- Why it works: [Opens a loop the reader must close by reading further]

**Option 5: Contrarian Headline** — Flip conventional wisdom
- Headline: [Statement that contradicts what the reader has been told]
- Sub-headline: [Brief evidence or authority that makes the contrarian claim credible]
- Why it works: [Pattern interrupt — stops the reader because it conflicts with their existing beliefs]

Recommend which headline to use based on the awareness level from Section 1.

### Section 4: Bullet Point Arsenal

15-20 fascination bullets — the hardest-working copy element in any sales letter. Each bullet is one line, specific, benefit-driven, and curiosity-inducing.

**Benefit Bullets (5)** — What they GET:
1. [Specific benefit + measurable outcome]
2. [Specific benefit + measurable outcome]
3. [Specific benefit + measurable outcome]
4. [Specific benefit + measurable outcome]
5. [Specific benefit + measurable outcome]

**Curiosity Bullets (5)** — What they'll LEARN:
1. [The [unexpected thing] that [causes surprising result] — page [X]]
2. [Why [common approach] actually [backfires] — and what to do instead]
3. [The [number]-[unit] method for [desired outcome] (hint: it's not [obvious thing])]
4. [What [authority figure/successful person] does differently about [topic] that nobody talks about]
5. [The single biggest mistake [audience] makes with [topic] — and the [easy/simple] fix]

**Proof Bullets (5)** — What others have ACHIEVED:
1. [How [name/type] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]]
2. [How [name/type] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]]
3. [How [name/type] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]]
4. [How [name/type] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]]
5. [How [name/type] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]]

**Fear Bullets (3-5)** — What they'll AVOID:
1. [The hidden [cost/danger/risk] of [common approach] that [bad outcome]]
2. [Why waiting [timeframe] could mean [specific negative consequence]]
3. [The [number] warning signs that [bad situation] is about to [get worse]]

Each bullet must pass the "so what?" test — if a reader can shrug and move on, the bullet is too weak. Rewrite until every bullet creates a micro-desire to know more.

### Section 5: Value Stack Section

Format the complete value stack for visual impact in the sales letter. If build-irresistible-offer output exists, pull directly from that blueprint.

Structure:
```
[Component 1 Name] ........................ Value: $[amount]
  → [One-sentence description of what this solves]

[Component 2 Name] ........................ Value: $[amount]
  → [One-sentence description of what this solves]

[Bonus 1 Name] ............................ Value: $[amount]
  → [One-sentence description of what this solves]

[Bonus 2 Name] ............................ Value: $[amount]
  → [One-sentence description of what this solves]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total Value: $[sum]
Your Investment Today: $[actual price]
You Save: $[difference]
```

Value stack rules:
- Individual component values must be defensible (what would someone pay for this alone?)
- Total value should be 5-10x the actual price — the gap is the deal
- Each component gets a name (named IP if possible) and a one-line benefit
- Progressive stacking — reveal one at a time, building the running total
- Price reveal comes AFTER the full stack is built (Step 13)

**The Positive Guarantee:**

**The Anti-Guarantee Conditions:**
[What they must do to qualify — shows good faith and filters out bad-faith refund seekers]

**The Consequence:**

## Rules

- This is ARCHITECTURE, not finished copy. Provide outlines, structures, key phrases, and section-by-section direction — not a 5,000-word polished sales letter. The user or a copywriter finishes it.
- Never fabricate testimonials, results, data, or credentials. Use only what the user provides. Flag gaps where proof is missing.
- Sequence matters more than eloquence. Follow the 21 steps in order. Do not rearrange blocks or skip steps unless the awareness level strategy explicitly calls for it.
- Every section must serve ONE psychological purpose. Do not try to prove AND sell AND close in the same section. One job per section.
- Headlines must be specific. "How to [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe] without [specific pain]" beats "Get Better Results" every time. No vague promises.
- Bullets are the hardest-working element of any sales letter. Invest heavily here. Each bullet must pass the "so what?" test.
- The P.S. is the second most-read element after the headline. Treat it as a standalone pitch that could sell the offer on its own.
- Price must NEVER appear before the value stack is built. Always anchor high, then reveal. The sequence is: offer → bonuses → value stack → price.
- Objections must be handled BEFORE the CTA, not after. By the time the reader hits the call to action, every major objection should already be addressed.
- Scarcity and urgency must be REAL. Fake countdown timers, "only 3 spots left" lies, and manufactured pressure destroy trust and credibility. If no real urgency exists, flag it as a gap rather than fabricating it.
- If brand-voice-router is active, apply the brand voice throughout all copy direction and key phrases.
- If humanize-ai-writing is available, apply its principles to all suggested copy and key phrases to ensure the output reads as natural and human.
- If extract-testimonial-stories output exists, use those formatted testimonials in Steps 8 and 20.
- If generate-persona-playbook output exists, match language, objection handling, and emotional triggers to the target persona profile.
- Ask Phase 1 questions ONE AT A TIME. Do not dump all 8 questions at once.

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- **Write the complete finished sales letter.** This produces the blueprint and architecture — the structural skeleton with direction for each section, not a polished 3,000-5,000 word final draft.
- **Design the visual layout of the page.** Typography, color, spacing, and visual hierarchy are separate concerns.
- **Create the landing page HTML or build the page.** Use funnel-landing-page-designer for visual implementation.
- **Build the offer itself.** The offer must exist before you write the letter. Use build-irresistible-offer to create or refine the offer.
- **Write email sequences that drive traffic to the letter.** Use build-email-story-engine for pre-launch and nurture sequences.
- **Handle payment processing, checkout design, or post-purchase flows.** Those are separate systems.
- **Replace a trained copywriter for high-stakes launches.** This blueprint dramatically accelerates the copywriting process, but for six- and seven-figure launches, a professional copywriter should execute from this architecture.
