# Design Pricing Architecture

## What This Skill Does

This skill determines optimal price points and designs complete pricing tier structures using the same
methodologies that pricing consultants charge $10K+ to deliver. It combines six proven pricing frameworks:
Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter, Gabor-Granger demand mapping, the Decoy Effect, Price Anchoring,
Conjoint Analysis principles, and the Hormozi Value Equation. The result is a fully justified pricing
architecture with specific dollar amounts, tier designs, and psychological pricing tactics.

Most founders and operators pick prices by gut feel or by copying competitors. This skill replaces
guesswork with structured analysis. It starts with the value your product delivers (not what it costs
you to make), maps the acceptable price range using established research methods, and then architects
tiers so the decoy effect and anchoring naturally guide buyers toward your target tier.

The output is a seven-section pricing architecture document covering value-based price foundation,
price sensitivity analysis, tier design with decoy strategy, anchoring strategy, psychological pricing
tactics, pricing page layout recommendations, and an A/B test plan to validate and optimize over time.

## Phase 1: Discovery Questions

Ask these questions ONE AT A TIME. Wait for the user's answer before proceeding to the next question.

**Q1: "What are you selling? Describe the core product or service."**

Understand the deliverable before anything else. We need to know what someone actually receives when they pay.

> If stuck, ask: "What does someone get when they pay you? Be specific about the deliverables — files, access, hours, outcomes, physical goods."

---

**Q2: "What does the competition charge for similar solutions?"**

Competitive pricing sets the market context. We are not going to copy these prices, but we need to know the landscape.

> If stuck, ask: "Find 3-5 competitors or alternatives your buyer might consider. What do they charge? What do they include at each price point?"

---

**Q3: "What result does your customer achieve? What is that result WORTH to them?"**

Value-based pricing starts here. The price should reflect the outcome, not the input.

> If stuck, ask: "If your customer gets the result you promise, how much is that worth in revenue gained, time saved, or pain avoided? Put a dollar figure on it — even a rough one."

---

**Q4: "How many pricing tiers do you want?"**

Three tiers (Good / Better / Best) is the default. Three is the magic number for the decoy effect. Only deviate with a specific reason.

> If stuck, ask: "Three tiers is usually optimal — it enables the decoy effect and avoids decision paralysis. Do you have a reason for more or fewer?"

---

**Q5: "What are ALL the features and deliverables you could include?"**

List everything from bare minimum to dream-state. We will sort them into tiers later.

> If stuck, ask: "Include everything: the bare minimum someone needs, the ideal package, and things you have considered adding but have not built yet. Think access levels, support tiers, bonus materials, speed of delivery, volume limits."

---

**Q6: "What is your current pricing (if any) and how is it working?"**

Existing pricing data is the strongest signal we have. If there is no current pricing, that is useful information too.

> If stuck, ask: "What are you charging now? Are people buying without hesitation, negotiating, or walking away? What is your conversion rate at the current price?"

---

**Q7: "Who is your target customer and what is their budget range?"**

The buyer's ability and willingness to pay defines the ceiling. A $50/month tool for solopreneurs lives in a different universe than a $5,000/month platform for enterprises.

> If stuck, ask: "What does your ideal customer typically spend on solutions like this? What is their annual income or budget for this category?"

---

**Q8: "What is your business model — one-time, subscription, retainer, or project-based?"**

The billing model changes everything about how we frame the price, design tiers, and apply discounts.

> If stuck, ask: "How do you currently get paid — or how do you want to get paid? Monthly recurring? Per project? Annual contract? One-time purchase?"

---

## Phase 2: Generate the Pricing Architecture

After gathering all eight answers, generate the complete pricing architecture across all seven sections below.

---

### Section 1: Value-Based Price Foundation

Using the customer's result value (Q3) and the Hormozi Value Equation:

**Value = (Dream Outcome x Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time to Result x Effort Required)**

| Factor | Rating (1-10) | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| **Dream Outcome** | /10 | How desirable is the end result? |
| **Likelihood of Achievement** | /10 | How certain is the customer to get the result? |
| **Time to Result** | /10 | How fast? (10 = instant, 1 = years) |
| **Effort Required** | /10 | How easy? (10 = effortless, 1 = grueling) |

**Value Score**: (Outcome x Likelihood) / (Time x Effort) = [calculated score]

**Value-Based Price Range**: The result is worth $[X] to the customer. At 10-20% of value delivered, the price range is $[low] - $[high].

**Justification**: Explain why this range is appropriate given the market context from Q2 and the buyer profile from Q7.

---

### Section 2: Price Sensitivity Analysis

Apply Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter logic using market data from Q2, current pricing from Q6, and value-based foundation from Section 1.

| Price Point | Value | How Determined |
|-------------|-------|----------------|
| **Point of Marginal Cheapness (PMC)** | $[X] | Below this, quality perception drops — buyers wonder what is wrong |
| **Point of Marginal Expensiveness (PME)** | $[X] | Above this, willingness to pay drops sharply |
| **Optimal Price Point (OPP)** | $[X] | Where purchase resistance is minimized |
| **Indifference Price Point (IDP)** | $[X] | Where equal numbers find it cheap vs. expensive |
| **Acceptable Price Range** | $[low] - $[high] | The window between PMC and PME |

If the user has survey data, use it to plot the four Van Westendorp curves (too cheap, bargain, expensive, too expensive) and find the intersections. If not, estimate using competitive analysis (Q2) + value-based foundation (Section 1) and state assumptions clearly.

**Gabor-Granger Demand Estimate**: At the optimal price point, estimated purchase probability is [X]%. At 20% above OPP, probability drops to [Y]%. This tells us [interpretation of price elasticity].

### Section 3: Tier Design with Decoy Architecture

Design the tier structure using the Decoy Effect (Huber, Payne, Puto, 1982). One tier is intentionally designed to make the target tier look like the obvious choice.

| | Tier 1: [Name] | Tier 2: [Name] **(TARGET)** | Tier 3: [Name] **(ANCHOR)** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Price** | $[X] | $[Y] | $[Z] |
| **Feature 1** | [check/cross] | [check] | [check] |
| **Feature 2** | [check/cross] | [check] | [check] |
| **Feature 3** | [cross] | [check] | [check] |
| **Feature 4** | [cross] | [cross] | [check] |
| **Feature 5** | [cross] | [cross] | [check] |
| **Target Buyer** | [persona] | [persona] | [persona] |
| **Role in Architecture** | Entry / Qualifier | Target (most buyers land here) | Anchor / Premium |

**Decoy Strategy Explanation:**

- **Which tier is the DECOY**: Identify it and explain the mechanism. The decoy is priced close to the target but offers significantly less value, creating asymmetric dominance. Alternatively, if using a three-tier anchor model, the premium tier's high price makes the target tier feel like a deal.
- **Price-to-feature ratio**: Show the specific math. Example: "Tier 1 gives 3 features for $49 ($16.33/feature). Tier 2 gives 7 features for $99 ($14.14/feature). Tier 2 is objectively better value per feature."
- **Expected distribution**: [X]% Tier 1, [Y]% Tier 2 (target), [Z]% Tier 3. Typical healthy distribution is 20/60/20 or 15/70/15 with the target tier capturing the majority.

**Conjoint Analysis Insight**: Based on the feature list (Q5) and buyer profile (Q7), the features driving the most willingness to pay are [feature A], [feature B], and [feature C]. These belong in the target tier to maximize its attractiveness.

---

### Section 4: Anchoring Strategy

Apply Tversky and Kahneman's anchoring effect to the pricing presentation.

- **Presentation Order**: Show [Tier Name] FIRST at $[highest price]. All subsequent prices are evaluated relative to this anchor. A $[anchor] premium tier makes $[target price] feel reasonable.
- **Anchor Price Justification**: The anchor is not arbitrary — it reflects [specific premium features/outcomes] and is priced at [X]% of the value delivered to that tier's buyer.
- **Price Framing**: Present the target tier price in multiple frames:
  - Full price: $[X]
  - Monthly equivalent: $[X/mo] (if annual)
  - Daily equivalent: $[X/day]
  - Per-unit equivalent: $[X] per [unit of value]
- **Comparison Anchors**: External references that make the price feel small. Examples: "Less than [common expense]" or "The cost of [relatable purchase]." Choose comparisons relevant to the buyer persona from Q7.

---

### Section 5: Psychological Pricing Tactics

Apply the tactics most relevant to this product and market.

| Tactic | Application | Expected Impact |
|--------|------------|-----------------|
| **Charm Pricing** ($X97 vs $X00) | Use for [specific tiers/contexts]. Effective for info products, courses, and consumer SaaS. Do NOT use for premium/enterprise positioning. | Increases conversions 2-8% in applicable contexts |
| **Round Numbers for Premium** | Use $[round number] for [premium tier]. Round numbers signal quality and reduce cognitive load for high-ticket decisions. | Increases perceived quality for premium offers |
| **Price-Value Gap** | Total value of included features: $[total]. Price: $[price]. Display the gap: "Get $[total] in value for just $[price]." | Creates urgency and justifies the purchase |
| **Annual Discount** | Offer [X] months free on annual billing. Display as "[X]% savings" — keep between 15-20%. | Increases annual plan adoption by 20-40% |
| **Payment Plans** | Break $[full price] into [N] payments of $[amount]. Emphasize the payment plan on the pricing page; show full price as alternative. | Reduces perceived risk; increases conversion on high-ticket |
| **Bundling** | Bundle [features X, Y, Z] together. These are worth more combined than sold separately because [reason]. | Increases average order value 15-30% |

**Which tactics to SKIP and why**: Not every tactic applies. State which ones to avoid for this specific product/market and explain the reasoning.

### Section 6: Pricing Page Layout Recommendations

Translate the pricing architecture into a page structure.

- **Visual Hierarchy**: Give Tier [N] ([Name]) the "Most Popular" or "Best Value" badge. Use a larger card, highlighted border, or contrasting background color to draw the eye.
- **Feature Comparison Table**: List features top-to-bottom from most to least valuable. Use checkmarks and crosses. Group features into categories if there are more than 8.
- **CTA Button Text**:
  - Tier 1: "[Low-commitment CTA]" (e.g., "Get Started", "Try Free")
  - Tier 2: "[Action-oriented CTA]" (e.g., "Start Growing", "Choose [Plan Name]")
  - Tier 3: "[Premium CTA]" (e.g., "Go Premium", "Contact Sales", "Unlock Everything")
- **Price Display**: Show the price large and bold. If using charm pricing, make the dollar portion large and the cents smaller. If showing a crossed-out comparison price, keep it subtle — do not make it look like a used car lot.
- **Savings Calculations**: Show "Save $[X]/year" or "Save [X]%" on annual plans. Place near the billing toggle, not buried in footnotes.
- **Monthly/Annual Toggle** (if subscription): Default to annual. Show both prices. Highlight the savings on annual clearly but honestly.
- **Social Proof Placement**: Place testimonials or customer counts near the target tier. Match testimonial personas to the tier's target buyer from Section 3.

## Rules

- Value-based pricing ALWAYS beats cost-plus pricing. Never start with "what does it cost me to deliver?" Start with "what is the result worth to the buyer?"
- Three tiers is the default. Only deviate with a specific, stated reason. Two tiers eliminates the decoy effect. Four or more tiers creates decision paralysis.
- The DECOY tier is not meant to sell. Its job is to make the TARGET tier look like the obvious choice. If the decoy is selling well, the architecture is broken.
- Present the highest-priced tier FIRST for anchoring, unless there is a documented strategic reason not to (e.g., freemium funnel where entry-tier awareness is the goal).
- Never recommend a price without justifying it. Every dollar amount needs a "because" — drawn from value analysis, competitive data, buyer profile, or pricing research.
- Price framing must be honest. "Per day" breakdowns and comparison anchors are legitimate. Misleading math or hidden fees are not.
- If the user has no market data, use competitive analysis (Q2) combined with value-based estimation (Section 1) to establish the range. State assumptions explicitly. Do not guess.
- Charm pricing ($97, $197, $497) works for info products, courses, and consumer SaaS. Round numbers ($500, $2,000, $10,000) work for premium services and enterprise. Apply the right one for the context.
- Payment plans reduce perceived risk but do not change the total price. Present both the payment plan and the full price clearly.
- Annual discounts should be 15-20% maximum. Discounts larger than 20% signal desperation or underpricing.
- If design-offer-ladder output exists, align pricing tiers with the offer ladder structure.
- If build-irresistible-offer output exists, use the value stack to justify price points and calculate the price-value gap.
- If generate-persona-playbook output exists, match tier targeting to the persona profiles.
- Apply brand-voice-router if active — pricing language should match the brand's tone.
- Ask Phase 1 questions ONE AT A TIME. Do not dump all eight questions at once.

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- **Build the product or define features from scratch.** Use build-irresistible-offer for value stack creation and feature definition.
- **Write sales copy for the pricing page.** Use build-conversion-sales-letter for persuasive copy.
- **Design the visual pricing page.** Use funnel-landing-page-designer for UI/UX layout and design.
- **Run actual customer surveys or collect primary research data.** This skill works with data you provide or estimates from competitive analysis. For real Van Westendorp or conjoint studies, use dedicated survey tools.
- **Handle billing infrastructure or payment processing.** Stripe, Paddle, billing logic, and payment integrations are out of scope.
- **Conduct formal conjoint analysis.** True conjoint requires specialized survey software with statistical analysis. This skill applies conjoint principles directionally.
- **Set prices for regulated industries** (healthcare, insurance, utilities) where pricing is subject to legal constraints beyond market dynamics.
