# Design Offer Ladder

## What This Skill Does

Takes your current offers — or lack thereof — and designs a complete product ladder where each tier solves a distinct problem, prices serve as filters, and every tier naturally leads to the next. The output prevents the two most common offer structure mistakes: price gaps that lose interested buyers, and overlapping tiers that cannibalize each other.

This is offer architecture, not sales page design. You'll get the structure, pricing logic, differentiation, upgrade paths, and cross-sell language. Building the sales pages and funnels for each tier is downstream work.

## Phase 1: Offer Structure Discovery

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Help them think through it — map out what they're already doing (even informally), identify where they lose people, and suggest common ladder patterns for their business type.

### The 7 Questions

**Q1: "What do you currently sell?"**
List every offer — name, price, what's included, how it's delivered. Include free offers (lead magnets, free calls, newsletters) — they're part of the ladder too. Also include anything you've thought about selling but haven't built yet.

*If stuck, ask:* "Start with what someone can buy from you today. Then tell me about anything free you give away. Then anything you've been considering adding."

**Q2: "Where are you losing people?"**
Where in your funnel do people drop off? Is there a price jump that kills momentum? Do people say "I'm not ready for that yet" or "that's more than I need"? Have you had people ask for something you don't offer?

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about the last 5 people who showed interest but didn't buy. What did they say? Were they not ready, not able to afford it, or not sure it was right for them?"

**Q3: "Who's your audience, and what are their readiness levels?"**
Some people are ready to go all-in on day one. Others need to dip a toe first. Others need to build trust over months. Describe the range of buyer readiness you see — from "just curious" to "ready to commit."

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about three types of people who find you: someone who just discovered you, someone who's been following for months, and someone who's ready to throw money at you. What does each person need?"

**Q4: "What's your flagship offer?"**
The one you'd build everything around if you could only sell one thing. What does it cost, what transformation does it deliver, and how is it delivered?

*If stuck, ask:* "If someone said 'I have unlimited budget and want your best work' — what would you sell them? That's your flagship."

**Q5: "What's the natural progression?"**
How does someone go from knowing nothing about you to being your best client? What steps do they take? What do they need to believe, learn, or experience at each step before they're ready for the next?

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about your best client's journey. How did they first find you? What did they consume or buy first? When did they decide to go deeper?"

**Q6: "What's your capacity?"**
Can you take unlimited customers at every tier, or are some tiers limited by your time? Do you have team members who deliver parts of the offer? How many high-touch clients can you realistically serve at once?

*If stuck, ask:* "If 100 people wanted your flagship offer tomorrow, could you serve them all? If not, what's the real number? And what about your lower-tier offers — are those unlimited?"

**Q7: "Are you launching or restructuring?"**
Building a ladder from scratch, or fixing gaps in an existing one? Are there any offers you're considering killing? Any you've been asked for but haven't built?

*If stuck, ask:* "Is this starting fresh, or are you trying to fix something that's not working in your current structure?"

## Phase 2: Generate the Offer Ladder Blueprint

After gathering all 7 answers, generate a structured document with these 7 sections.

### Section 1: Current State Diagnosis

Map the current offer structure visually:

```
FREE          $XX          $XXX          $X,XXX          $XX,XXX
|___________|____________|_____________|________________|
[Offer A]    [Offer B]                  [Offer C]
                         ← GAP →
```

Identify:
- **Price gaps** — jumps greater than 5x between adjacent tiers (the "$21 to $4,500 problem")
- **Cannibalization risks** — tiers that overlap in what they deliver (same job-to-be-done at different prices)
- **Dead zones** — audience segments with no matching offer (people who are willing to spend but don't have an option)
- **Capacity bottlenecks** — tiers where the user's time limits revenue

### Section 2: Recommended Offer Ladder

For each tier, from lowest to highest price:

**[Tier Name] — $[Price]**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Job-to-be-Done** | The specific problem this tier solves (not the transformation — the immediate need) |
| **What's Included** | Deliverables, access, components |
| **What's NOT Included** | Explicitly excluded (prevents cannibalization) |
| **Delivery Method** | Self-serve, group, 1:1, async, hybrid |
| **Filtering Function** | What the price point selects for (curiosity, commitment, urgency, investment capacity) |
| **Capacity** | Unlimited, limited (how many), or by-application |
| **Upgrade Trigger** | The moment someone realizes they need the next tier |

### Section 3: Differentiation Table

| Tier | Price | Job-to-be-Done | Includes | Does NOT Include | Upgrade Trigger |
|------|-------|---------------|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Free | $0 | [job] | [items] | [exclusions] | [trigger] |
| Entry | $XX | [job] | [items] | [exclusions] | [trigger] |
| Core | $XXX | [job] | [items] | [exclusions] | [trigger] |
| Premium | $X,XXX | [job] | [items] | [exclusions] | [trigger] |

The "Does NOT Include" column is the most important. This is what prevents cannibalization.

### Section 4: Upgrade Path Strategy

For each tier transition (A → B, B → C, etc.):

**From [Tier A] to [Tier B]:**
- **Why they upgrade:** What happens that makes Tier A insufficient
- **Cross-sell language (on Tier A):** "If you already know X but need help with Y, that's [Tier B]."
- **Cross-sell language (on Tier B):** "Already have X sorted? You might be looking for [Tier A] instead."
- **Anti-cannibalization script:** What to say when someone asks "Can't I just get the cheaper one?"

### Section 5: Pricing Psychology

For each tier, explain:
- **Why this price point:** What it signals about the buyer and the value
- **Filtering function:** How the price selects the right people (e.g., "$497/mo stings if you waste it — that discomfort IS the accountability")
- **Anchoring effect:** How the full ladder makes each tier feel like a deal relative to others
- **Comparison point:** What else they could spend this money on (the "instead of" frame)

### Section 6: Implementation Notes

**Launch Sequence:**
Which tier to launch or promote first, and why. Usually: validate the flagship, then build down to capture the people you're losing.

**Landing Page Strategy:**
- Each tier needs its own landing page for paid traffic (one CTA per page)
- Create a comparison/overview page for organic traffic (Work With Me / Products page)
- Don't send cold ad traffic to a page with multiple offers — it creates decision paralysis

**Pilot Pricing:**
For any new tier being introduced:
- Founding customer discount (60-70% off) for first 5-10 customers
- Or 90-day free pilot with defined conversion terms
- Lock in the founding rate for 12 months to reward early adopters
- Include feedback obligations in pilot agreements

### Section 7: Gaps & Recommendations

- **Missing tiers:** Audience segments that still don't have an offer
- **Tiers to consider killing:** Low value, high maintenance, or cannibalizing something better
- **Price adjustments:** Tiers that are too close or too far apart
- **Future tiers:** Natural extensions of the ladder (community, mastermind, licensing, certification)
- **Cross-sell optimization:** Where the handoff language between tiers needs work

## Rules

- **Each tier must have a DISTINCT job-to-be-done.** "Accountability" and "strategic partnership" are different jobs. "Basic coaching" and "premium coaching" are the same job at different depths — that's cannibalization.
- **Price jumps between adjacent tiers should be 3-5x maximum.** $21 → $497 is fine (24x but there's a clear job difference). $21 → $4,500 with nothing between is a gap that loses people.
- **The "Does NOT Include" column is as important as "Includes."** This is the anti-cannibalization mechanism. If you can't articulate what a tier excludes, it's too close to another tier.
- **Never recommend a tier just to fill a price point.** Every tier must solve a real problem for a real segment. A $199 tier that exists only because "$21 to $497 felt like a big jump" is filler.
- **Pricing should reflect value AND filtering function.** The price isn't just about what something is worth — it's about who it attracts. A $497/mo accountability program that "stings if you waste it" filters for commitment. That's a feature.
- **Free offers are part of the ladder.** Lead magnets, free calls, newsletters, and free tools all have a job-to-be-done and should feed into paid tiers with clear upgrade triggers.
- **Don't design sales pages or funnels.** This skill produces the offer architecture. Copy, design, and funnel structure are downstream.
- **If brand-voice-router is active,** apply detected voice to all cross-sell language and scripts.
- **One blueprint per ladder.** If the user has multiple businesses with separate ladders, produce separate blueprints. Don't mix them.
- **Acknowledge when a tier should be killed.** If an offer is low-value, high-maintenance, and cannibalizes something better — say so directly. Don't preserve tiers out of politeness.

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- Build individual offer blueprints (use build-irresistible-offer for that)
- Write sales page copy or design funnels
- Create pricing pages or checkout flows
- Set up payment processing
- Design email sequences between tiers (use create-tag-based-funnel-system for that)
- Build the products themselves
