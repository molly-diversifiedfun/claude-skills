# Build Email Story Engine

## What This Skill Does

Designs the complete email content architecture for a business using story-driven sequences. This covers two critical phases of subscriber communication: the initial onboarding experience (Soap Opera Sequence) that transforms a cold subscriber into an engaged buyer over 5 days, and the ongoing broadcast strategy (Seinfeld Email Method) that keeps subscribers opening, reading, and clicking every day after that.

This skill encodes frameworks from Russell Brunson (DotCom Secrets), Andre Chaperon (Autoresponder Madness), Ben Settle, Dean Jackson, and Jay Abraham. It builds your Attractive Character profile, maps your origin story into a dramatic 5-email arc, generates 30 broadcast topic seeds, creates belief-breaking mini-series for your audience's top objections, and designs re-engagement emails for cold subscribers.

This is the CONTENT counterpart to create-tag-based-funnel-system, which handles the automation, tags, and state management. This skill produces what goes inside the emails. That skill produces the machinery that sends them at the right time to the right people.

## Phase 1: Story Engine Discovery

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Use the helper prompt to reframe. Most people have the raw material for great emails — they just haven't organized it into story form yet.

### The 7 Questions

**Q1: "What do you sell, and what's the first offer new subscribers should see?"**
When someone joins your email list, the Soap Opera Sequence ends with a call to action. What is that CTA pointing to? This is the product, program, or offer the entire 5-email arc builds toward.

*If stuck, ask:* "When someone joins your email list, what do you ultimately want them to buy? What's the first step toward that? Even if it's a $7 tripwire or a free strategy call — that's the first offer."

**Q2: "What's your origin story — the moment you discovered your solution?"**
This becomes the backbone of the Soap Opera Sequence. The best origin stories follow a pattern: you were struggling, you tried everything, nothing worked, then you had a breakthrough moment (the Epiphany), and everything changed. The more specific and emotional, the better.

*If stuck, ask:* "What were you struggling with before you found your approach? What was the lowest point? What was the turning point — the specific moment things clicked? What happened in the weeks and months after that breakthrough?"

**Q3: "What are 3 stories from your life or business that teach a lesson related to what you sell?"**
These become Seinfeld email templates and Epiphany Bridge stories. They don't need to be dramatic — everyday observations work. A conversation at a coffee shop. A mistake you made on Tuesday. Something your kid said that made you think about business differently.

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about a time you learned something the hard way. A mistake you made that cost you money or time. A surprising success you didn't expect. A conversation that changed how you think about your work. Those are your stories."

**Q4: "What's your Attractive Character identity?"**
Every email personality falls into one of four archetypes:
- **The Leader** — Been there, done that, follow me. You speak from authority and proven results.
- **The Adventurer** — I'm on a journey, come along. You share what you're learning in real time.
- **The Reporter** — I interview experts and share what I learn. You curate wisdom from others.
- **The Reluctant Hero** — I didn't want to do this, but I had to. You were dragged into your expertise by circumstance.

*If stuck, ask:* "When you share your expertise, do you lead from authority (Leader), from curiosity (Adventurer), from research (Reporter), or from reluctant experience (Reluctant Hero)? Which feels most natural when you talk to customers?"

**Q5: "What are the top 3 objections or false beliefs your audience holds?"**
These become belief-breaking email content. Every purchase decision requires breaking through false beliefs — about themselves, about your method, and about external factors. What do people believe that stops them from buying or succeeding?

*If stuck, ask:* "What do people say when they decide NOT to buy? What excuses do they make? What do they believe is true that actually isn't? What misconceptions do you correct over and over?"

**Q6: "What's your email sending cadence and comfort level?"**
The Seinfeld Method works best daily, but only if you can sustain it. Daily builds the strongest habit loop (subscribers expect and open your emails). 3x/week is a solid middle ground. Weekly is the minimum for maintaining engagement.

*If stuck, ask:* "How often do you currently email your list? How often would you LIKE to? Be honest about what you can sustain for 6 months without burning out."

**Q7: "What's your brand voice — how do you talk to your audience?"**
Your emails need to sound like you, not like a marketing textbook. Voice is the single biggest factor in whether someone reads past the first line. Casual or formal? Profanity or clean? Long sentences or punchy fragments? Humor or straight talk?

*If stuck, ask:* "If you were texting a friend who needed your help, how would you write? What words would you never use? What words do you overuse? Show me a message or post you've written that feels most like 'you.'"

## Phase 2: Generate the Email Story Engine

After gathering all 7 answers, generate a complete email content architecture with these 6 sections.

### Section 1: Attractive Character Profile

Build the persona that will inhabit every email.

**Identity Type:** State the archetype from Q4 and explain how it shapes email tone and structure.

**Backstory Summary:** A 3-4 sentence version of their origin story (from Q2) written in the voice established in Q7. This is the "about me" paragraph that can be referenced across emails.

**3 Signature Parables:** For each story from Q3, create a titled parable:

| Parable Title | Story Summary | Lesson It Teaches | Best Used In |
|--------------|---------------|-------------------|--------------|
| [Memorable title] | [2-sentence summary] | [Core takeaway] | [Soap Opera / Seinfeld / Belief-Breaking] |

**Character Flaws to Reveal:** List 2-3 genuine vulnerabilities or imperfections to share in emails. Flaws create relatability. Examples: impatience, past failures, times they were wrong, bad habits they still fight.

**Polarity Positions:** List 3-5 strong opinions that will attract ideal subscribers and repel the wrong ones. Polarity creates engagement. Examples: contrarian industry takes, unpopular methods they advocate, things they refuse to do.

**Voice Guidelines:** A reference card for email copy:
- Sentence length tendency (short/mixed/long)
- Vocabulary level (casual/conversational/formal)
- Humor style (none/dry/self-deprecating/sarcastic)
- Profanity level (none/mild/moderate)
- Signature phrases or verbal tics to use
- Words and phrases to NEVER use

### Section 2: Soap Opera Sequence (5 Emails)

Design the complete 5-email onboarding arc. Show the narrative spine first, then detail each email.

**Narrative Arc Map:**
```
Email 1 (Set the Stage) → Email 2 (High Drama) → Email 3 (Epiphany) → Email 4 (Hidden Benefits) → Email 5 (Urgency + CTA)
[Rapport + Promise]      [Obstacle + Tension]    [Breakthrough + Emotion]  [Visualization + Proof]   [Action + Deadline]
     ↓ open loop              ↓ open loop              ↓ open loop              ↓ open loop              ↓ close
```

**For each email (1-5), provide:**

**Email [#]: [Title] — [Emotional Goal]**
- **Send timing:** Day [#] after opt-in
- **Subject lines** (3 options, ranked by curiosity strength):
  1. [Strongest curiosity driver]
  2. [Story-based subject]
  3. [Benefit-based subject]
- **Open with** (the hook — first 2 lines that compel reading):
  > [Exact opening copy]
- **Story arc:** [3-5 sentence summary of what happens in this email — the narrative beats]
- **Open loop** (how the email ends — the unresolved thread driving opens of the next email):
  > [Exact closing copy that creates the open loop]
- **Zeigarnik trigger:** [The specific unanswered question the reader carries to the next email]
- **Emotional goal:** [What the reader should FEEL after reading — one word plus explanation]
- **CTA:** [None for Emails 1-4; hard CTA with link placement guidance for Email 5]
- **Word count target:** 300-500 words

**Email-by-email framework:**

| Email | Role | Story Element | Open Loop Type | Emotional Target |
|-------|------|--------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 1 — Set the Stage | Introduce yourself, build rapport, set expectations | Backstory beginning — who you were BEFORE | "Tomorrow I'll tell you about the moment everything changed..." | Curiosity + Connection |
| 2 — High Drama | Present obstacles using dramatic storytelling | The struggle — what went wrong, what you tried | "But I haven't told you the worst part yet..." | Tension + Empathy |
| 3 — The Epiphany | Reveal the big breakthrough | The specific moment of discovery — what clicked | "And that's when I realized there was something ELSE nobody talks about..." | Relief + Excitement |
| 4 — Hidden Benefits | Detail unexpected secondary benefits | Results beyond the obvious — what else changed | "But the real question is: will it work for YOU? Tomorrow I'll show you..." | Desire + Visualization |
| 5 — Urgency + CTA | Direct call to action with deadline/scarcity | Full circle — connect story to offer | No open loop — close all threads, drive action | Urgency + Confidence |

**Seinfeld Email Structure Template:**
Every broadcast email follows this skeleton:
1. **Hook** (1-2 sentences) — Drop into the middle of the story
2. **Story** (100-200 words) — Tell it with specific details, dialogue, sensory language
3. **Pivot** (1 sentence) — "And that's exactly like..." or "Which reminded me..."
4. **Lesson** (2-3 sentences) — The insight or principle
5. **CTA** (1-2 sentences) — Link to offer, content, or reply prompt
6. **Total:** 200-400 words

### Section 4: Belief-Breaking Email Mini-Series

For each of the 3 false beliefs from Q5, design a 3-email arc that systematically dismantles the belief and replaces it with a new one.

**False Belief #[1]: "[State the belief]"**

**Email 1 of 3: Name It and Validate It**
- **Subject line options** (3):
- **Approach:** Acknowledge the belief. Show you understand WHY they believe it. Make them feel seen, not attacked. Use phrases like "I used to believe this too" or "Most people think..."
- **Story element:** A moment when you or someone else held this exact belief
- **End with:** A crack in the belief — one small piece of evidence that it might not be true
- **Emotional goal:** Validated but slightly uncertain

**Email 2 of 3: The Epiphany Bridge**
- **Subject line options** (3):
- **Approach:** Tell a story (yours or a customer's) where this belief was proven wrong. Not with data — with narrative. The reader experiences the epiphany through the story.
- **Story element:** The specific moment the old belief broke — what happened, what was said, what changed
- **End with:** The new belief stated clearly. "The truth is..."
- **Emotional goal:** Shifted perspective, ready for proof

**Email 3 of 3: New Belief + Proof + CTA**
- **Subject line options** (3):
- **Approach:** Reinforce the new belief with evidence — testimonials, results, logic. Then connect directly to the offer as the vehicle for acting on the new belief.
- **Proof elements:** [Specify what proof to include — testimonial, case study, data point, demonstration]
- **CTA:** Direct link to offer with framing tied to the new belief
- **Emotional goal:** Conviction and readiness to act

Repeat for all 3 false beliefs (9 emails total).

### Section 5: Re-Engagement and Win-Back Emails

**The 9-Word Email (Dean Jackson):**
For subscribers who haven't opened or clicked in 60+ days.

- **Subject line:** [First name only — nothing else]
- **Body:** [A single question, approximately 9 words, customized to their business. Format: "Are you still looking for [specific outcome]?"]
- **Formatting:** Plain text. No images. No links. No signature block. No HTML formatting. It must look like a personal email from a real person.
- **Goal:** Get a reply. That's it. The reply re-starts the conversation.

**The Breakup Email:**
For subscribers who haven't engaged in 90+ days, as a list-cleaning tool.

- **Subject line options** (3, using loss aversion):
- **Body approach:** "I'm cleaning my list and removing people who aren't interested anymore. If you still want to hear from me, click [this link]. If not, no hard feelings — I'll remove you in 48 hours."
- **Mechanism:** Loss aversion — people act to avoid losing something they already have (their subscription). The deadline creates urgency without being salesy.
- **Formatting:** Short. Direct. No storytelling in this one — pure clarity.

**The Story Return:**
For subscribers who stopped engaging but didn't unsubscribe. A compelling story-driven email that reminds them why they signed up.

- **Subject line options** (3):
- **Story approach:** Tell a new story that connects to the subscriber's original reason for joining. Reference the lead magnet or original promise. Show what's new or different since they last engaged.
- **CTA:** Low-friction — reply, read a post, watch a short video. Not a purchase.
- **Emotional goal:** "Oh right, this is why I subscribed."

## Rules

- Every email must have ONE clear purpose — don't try to do 3 things in one email
- Open loops are MANDATORY in the Soap Opera Sequence — never close all threads before Email 5
- Soap Opera Sequence emails should be 300-500 words — not novels, not tweets
- Seinfeld emails should be 200-400 words — shorter, punchier, daily-friendly
- Never fabricate testimonials, results, or data in email copy — flag where real proof is needed and leave a placeholder
- Subject lines must be specific and curiosity-driven — no clickbait, no ALL CAPS, no emoji spam
- The first line of every email must hook — no "Hope you're doing well" or "Happy Monday" or "I wanted to reach out"
- CTA links should appear 2-3 times in selling emails (top, middle, bottom)
- Brand voice consistency is non-negotiable — every email should sound like the same person wrote it
- If brand-voice-router is active, apply its voice profile to all generated copy
- If humanize-ai-writing is available, run all email copy through it before finalizing
- If generate-persona-playbook output exists, use the persona's language patterns, pain points, and desires in email content
- Stories must feel REAL — specific details, real emotions, actual incidents, named places, concrete numbers. No generic "I once met a man who..." or "Imagine a world where..."
- The 9-Word Email must be plain text with zero formatting — it must look like a personal email, not a marketing email
- Every Soap Opera Sequence email must end with an explicit Zeigarnik trigger — an unanswered question the reader MUST know the answer to
- Belief-breaking sequences must validate the false belief BEFORE attacking it — never start by telling people they're wrong
