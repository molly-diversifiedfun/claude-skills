# Design Onboarding Sequence

## What This Skill Does

Takes the most neglected stage of the customer lifecycle -- the post-purchase experience -- and architects a complete onboarding system that moves new buyers from "I just paid" to "this was worth every penny." Uses the Bowling Alley Framework (Ramli John), the EUREKA Framework, Time-to-Value methodology, and behavioral email sequencing to design the straight-line path from purchase to activation, with bumpers at every point where customers typically fall off.

Most solopreneurs spend all their energy on acquisition and zero on activation. The result: high refund rates, low engagement, no testimonials, no referrals, and a leaky bucket that no amount of marketing can fill. This skill fixes the bucket. It identifies the single "aha moment" that correlates with retention, maps the critical path to reach it, designs behavioral email sequences that nudge users toward that moment, builds proactive support systems that intervene before people churn, and creates a health scoring model so you always know which customers need attention.

This is not email copywriting. This is onboarding architecture -- the strategy and structure that the copy serves.

## Phase 1: Discovery Questions

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Don't just repeat it. Help them think through it -- suggest options based on what they've already told you, offer examples from common patterns, or reframe the question. The goal is to get a real answer, not a placeholder.

**If the user truly can't answer one** (e.g., they have no data yet for a new product), note it as a gap and keep moving. Flag it in Section 7 of the blueprint.

### The 7 Questions

**Q1: "What did the customer just buy, and what does success look like for them?"**
Not what the product IS -- what it DOES for them. The transformation they expect. What would make them look back in 30 days and say "best purchase I've made"?

*If stuck, ask:* "Imagine your best customer 30 days after buying. What have they accomplished? What would they tell a friend?"

**Q2: "What's the 'aha moment' -- the single action where they first experience real value?"**
This is the most important question. Everything in onboarding should drive toward this moment. Not the full transformation -- the first taste of it. The moment where the purchase shifts from hope to evidence.

*If stuck, ask:* "What's the first thing that makes someone say 'oh, this was worth it'? Not the full transformation -- the first taste. For Slack it was sending 2,000 messages. For Dropbox it was saving the first file. What's yours?"

**Q3: "What are the 3-5 steps between purchase and the aha moment?"**
Map the critical path. What must they DO, in order, to get from "I just bought this" to "oh, this works"?

*If stuck, ask:* "After they buy, what do they need to DO first? Then what? Walk me through it step by step, as if you were sitting next to a new customer guiding them."

**Q4: "Where do people typically get stuck or drop off?"**
Identify the friction points. This is where the bumpers go. Every drop-off point without an intervention is a hole in your bucket.

*If stuck, ask:* "What questions do new customers ask most? What support tickets do you get in the first week? Where do people go silent? If you don't have data yet, where do YOU think they'd get confused?"

**Q5: "What does your current onboarding look like?"**
Could be nothing, could be a welcome email, could be a full sequence. Honest assessment of the current state.

*If stuck, ask:* "What happens right now after someone buys? What emails do they get? What instructions? Be honest -- even 'nothing' is a valid answer. Most solopreneurs are starting from zero here, and that's fine."

**Q6: "What's the delivery format -- digital product, service, coaching, SaaS, physical?"**
This shapes the onboarding medium. A SaaS product can use in-app nudges plus emails. A course is email-heavy. Coaching requires expectation-setting about the working relationship, not just deliverables.

*If stuck, ask:* "How does the customer actually receive and use what they bought? Do they log in somewhere? Download something? Show up to a call? Walk me through the mechanics."

**Q7: "What would make someone cancel, refund, or stop using it in the first 30 days?"**
Identify churn triggers. These are the gutter balls -- the reasons people abandon before they reach the aha moment.

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about the customers who didn't work out. What went wrong? When did they disengage? If you haven't had customers yet, what are you most worried about?"

## Phase 2: Generate the Onboarding Blueprint

After gathering all 7 answers, generate a structured document with these 7 sections. Use the user's answers as raw material -- don't just repeat their words back. Synthesize, sharpen, and fill gaps using the Bowling Alley Framework, EUREKA Framework, and Time-to-Value methodology.

### Section 1: Activation Map

The Bowling Alley Framework applied to their product. The user is the bowling ball. The aha moment is the pins. Onboarding elements are the bumpers. Map the straight-line path and identify where bumpers are needed.

**Format as a visual path:**

```
PURCHASE
  |
  v
[Step 1: ____________] ---> Drop-off risk: ____________
  |                         Bumper: ____________
  v
[Step 2: ____________] ---> Drop-off risk: ____________
  |                         Bumper: ____________
  v
[Step 3: ____________] ---> Drop-off risk: ____________
  |                         Bumper: ____________
  v
[Step 4: ____________] ---> Drop-off risk: ____________
  |                         Bumper: ____________
  v
*** AHA MOMENT: ____________ ***
```

Then summarize:

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **The Aha Moment** | [Specific action/experience from Q2] |
| **Time-to-Value Target** | [X hours/days to reach aha moment] |
| **Critical Path** | Step 1 -> Step 2 -> Step 3 -> ... -> Aha Moment |
| **Biggest Drop-off Risk** | [The step where most people fall off] |
| **Most Important Bumper** | [The intervention that prevents the biggest drop-off] |

90% of users who don't understand value within the first week will churn. The TTV target should reflect this urgency.

### Section 2: Onboarding Email Sequence

5-7 emails triggered by behavior (not just time). Behavioral triggers always beat time-based triggers. "User hasn't logged in for 3 days" is more useful than "it's day 3."

For each email, provide:

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Email #** | [Number and name] |
| **Trigger** | [What triggers this email -- time-based OR behavior-based] |
| **Subject Line** | [2-3 options] |
| **Purpose** | [Single goal of this email] |
| **Key Message** | [The one thing they need to know/do] |
| **CTA** | [The one action you want them to take] |
| **Tone** | [Supportive, celebratory, concerned, etc.] |

**Standard sequence (adapt to the product):**

- **Email 1: Welcome + ONE Next Step** -- Sent immediately after purchase. This is the most important email you will ever send. It sets expectations, reduces buyer's remorse, and tells them exactly what to do first. ONE action, not five.
- **Email 2: Quick Win Guidance** -- Day 1-2, or triggered by first login/access. Help them get their first small result. Hand-hold through the first step on the critical path.
- **Email 3: Social Proof Story** -- Day 2-3. Show someone like them succeeding. Not a generic testimonial -- a story that mirrors their situation and shows the path forward.
- **Email 4: "Are You Stuck?"** -- Triggered by inactivity (no login, no progress after 3 days). Offer genuine help, not guilt. This email should feel like a friend checking in, not a brand nudging for engagement metrics.
- **Email 5: Undiscovered Value** -- Day 5-7. Highlight a feature, benefit, or use case they haven't explored yet. Reframe value from a different angle.
- **Email 6: Progress Celebration OR Re-engagement** -- Day 7-10, behavioral. If they've made progress: celebrate it. If they haven't: offer a different path to the aha moment. This is a fork based on behavior.
- **Email 7: Value Reinforcement + Next Milestone** -- Day 14. Reinforce how far they've come. Point to the next milestone. For retained users, this transitions from onboarding to ongoing engagement.

### Section 3: Milestone Celebration System

Define 3-5 customer milestones and how to celebrate each. Celebrating progress reinforces behavior and creates positive emotional association with the product.

| Milestone | What It Means | How to Celebrate | Channel |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| First [action] | They engaged with the product | Congratulations email with next step | Email |
| Completed [setup/module] | They're activated | Share what's possible now + social proof | Email / In-app |
| First [result/outcome] | They experienced value | Ask for feedback or testimonial | Email |
| [X] days consistently active | They're forming a habit | Unlock bonus content or next tier preview | Email |
| Completed [full program/goal] | They achieved the transformation | Celebrate + referral ask + upgrade path | Email / Personal |

**Rules for celebrations:**
- Celebrations must be earned, not scheduled. Trigger on behavior, not calendar.
- Each celebration should include what's NEXT -- don't leave them without direction after a win.
- The "first result" milestone is the ideal time to ask for a testimonial. They're feeling the value. Don't wait.

### Section 4: Proactive Support System

Based on Q4 (drop-off points), design proactive interventions. Proactive support beats reactive support -- intervene before they ask, before they get frustrated, before they go silent.

For each friction point identified:

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **The Problem** | What goes wrong at this step |
| **The Signal** | How you know it's happening (observable behavior -- no login, incomplete setup, support ticket pattern) |
| **The Intervention** | What to send or do (email, personal outreach, resource, video walkthrough) |
| **The Timing** | When to intervene (specific trigger window -- don't wait too long) |
| **Escalation** | What to do if the intervention doesn't work |

Design at least one intervention for every drop-off point identified in Q4. If there are more drop-off points than interventions, flag the gap in Section 7.

### Section 5: Health Score Definition

Create a simple customer health score using OBSERVABLE metrics -- not guesses, not feelings, not "they seem engaged." Binary or numeric indicators that can be tracked.

| Indicator | Weight | Healthy (Green) | At-Risk (Yellow) | Critical (Red) |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| [Metric 1: e.g., login frequency] | X% | [threshold] | [threshold] | [threshold] |
| [Metric 2: e.g., feature adoption] | X% | [threshold] | [threshold] | [threshold] |
| [Metric 3: e.g., milestone completion] | X% | [threshold] | [threshold] | [threshold] |
| [Metric 4: e.g., support interactions] | X% | [threshold] | [threshold] | [threshold] |

**Action triggers:**
- **Healthy (green):** No intervention needed. Continue standard sequence.
- **At-Risk (yellow):** Trigger check-in email or low-touch outreach. Offer help, not pressure.
- **Critical (red):** Trigger personal outreach or support escalation. This person is about to churn -- act now.

Weights must total 100%. Thresholds must be specific and measurable. Adapt indicators to the delivery format (SaaS metrics differ from course metrics differ from coaching metrics).

### Section 6: 30-Day Success Checklist

A simple, shareable checklist the customer can follow. This reduces cognitive load -- they don't have to figure out what to do next.

**Week 1: Foundation**
- [ ] [Step 1 -- immediate post-purchase action]
- [ ] [Step 2 -- first engagement with core product]
- [ ] [Step 3 -- complete initial setup or first module]

**Week 2: Activation**
- [ ] [Step 4 -- reach the aha moment]
- [ ] [Step 5 -- deepen engagement]

**Week 3: Momentum**
- [ ] [Step 6 -- build on initial success]
- [ ] [Step 7 -- explore secondary features/value]

**Week 4: Retention**
- [ ] [Step 8 -- milestone review and reflection]
- [ ] [Step 9 -- set next goal or engage with community]
- [ ] [Step 10 -- share feedback or results]

**Rules:**
- Each item must be a specific, completable action -- not "engage with the content"
- The aha moment should occur no later than Week 2
- The checklist should be deliverable as a standalone asset (PDF, email, in-app)

### Section 7: Onboarding Gaps & Recommendations

Audit the complete onboarding blueprint against these criteria and flag anything missing or weak:

- **Time-to-Value assessment:** Is TTV too long? If the aha moment takes more than 7 days to reach, recommend how to compress it. Can any steps be combined, automated, or eliminated?
- **Missing bumpers:** Are there drop-off points from Q4 that don't have corresponding interventions? Flag each one with a recommended bumper.
- **Celebration gaps:** Are there milestones without recognition? Uncelebrated progress is a missed retention opportunity.
- **Feedback loops:** Is the onboarding system learning from early customers? Are there mechanisms to capture what's working and what isn't? If not, recommend specific feedback collection points.
- **Behavioral vs. time-based ratio:** How many triggers are behavioral vs. calendar-based? If more than half are time-based, recommend behavioral alternatives.
- **Upgrade path:** Does onboarding set up the next purchase naturally, without hard-selling? The best time to hint at the next tier is after a success milestone -- not during activation.
- **Unanswered questions:** Any questions from Phase 1 the user couldn't answer -- list them here with recommendations for how to get the data.
- **Companion skill gaps:** If the user hasn't defined their offer (build-irresistible-offer) or launch sequence (design-launch-sequence), note how those gaps affect onboarding design.

For each gap, recommend a specific, actionable step to close it.

## Rules

- The aha moment is the SINGLE most important element -- everything in the blueprint serves getting users there faster
- Behavioral triggers over time-based triggers, always. "User hasn't logged in for 3 days" beats "it's day 3"
- Every email has ONE purpose and ONE CTA -- don't overwhelm new customers who are already processing a purchase decision
- Celebrate progress, don't just push action. Recognition reinforces behavior and creates positive emotional loops
- Never assume the customer knows what to do next -- spell it out with specific, concrete next steps
- The first email after purchase is the most important email you will ever send. It sets expectations and reduces buyer's remorse. Get it right.
- Onboarding is NOT upselling. Don't pitch the next tier until they've succeeded at the current tier. Premature upselling during onboarding destroys trust.
- "Are you stuck?" emails should offer genuine help, not guilt trips. The tone is a friend checking in, not a brand demanding engagement.
- Proactive support beats reactive support -- intervene before they ask, before frustration builds
- If the product is a service or coaching: the onboarding must set expectations about the working relationship, not just deliverables. Communication cadence, response times, what's expected of the client.
- If generate-persona-playbook output exists, match onboarding language and examples to the persona's psychology, vocabulary, and motivation patterns
- If brand-voice-router is active, apply the detected brand voice to all email subject lines, key messages, and milestone celebrations
- Apply humanize-ai-writing to all copy output -- onboarding emails that read like AI wrote them undermine trust at the most fragile moment in the customer relationship
- Health scores must use OBSERVABLE metrics -- login frequency, feature usage, milestone completion, support interactions. Never score based on assumptions about how the customer "feels"
- One onboarding blueprint per product. If the user has multiple products, each gets its own blueprint.
- Never fabricate testimonials, case studies, or social proof for the email sequence. If proof doesn't exist yet, flag it as a gap and recommend how to collect it.

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- Design the product itself (the thing being onboarded into)
- Write full email copy (provides architecture, subject lines, key messages, and CTAs -- not complete email drafts)
- Build the tech stack (email platform selection, CRM setup, automation tooling)
- Handle billing, refunds, or payment processing
- Create upsell or cross-sell sequences (that's a different stage -- onboarding ends when activation is complete)
- Run customer interviews or surveys (recommends where to collect feedback, but doesn't execute it)
- Design the pre-purchase experience (that's design-launch-sequence and build-irresistible-offer territory)
- Build win-back sequences for already-churned customers (that's build-win-back-system)
