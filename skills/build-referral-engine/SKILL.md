# Build Referral Engine

## What This Skill Does

Takes any business and builds a complete referral engine — the systematic process that turns happy customers into a predictable growth channel and identifies strategic partners who can introduce you to pre-qualified audiences at scale. Referred customers have 16% higher lifetime value and 4x higher conversion rates, yet most solopreneurs have ZERO systematic referral process. This skill fixes that.

This is not about slapping a "refer a friend, get $50" widget on your website. This is referral architecture — identifying peak satisfaction moments, crafting asks that make referrers look like heroes (not salespeople), building Dream 100 partner pipelines with multi-touch outreach sequences, and designing host-beneficiary deals where the host takes zero risk. The output is a complete blueprint with scripts, templates, partner strategies, flywheel metrics, and community orbit plans.

The skill draws on Jay Abraham's host-beneficiary model, Chet Holmes' Dream 100 strategy, Jim Collins' flywheel concept, Cialdini's reciprocity and unity principles, the Orbit Model for community engagement, and NPS-based referral timing. It synthesizes these into a single actionable engine tailored to your business.

## Phase 1: Discovery Questions

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Don't just repeat it. Help them think through it — suggest options based on what they've already told you, offer examples from common patterns, or reframe the question. The goal is to get a real answer, not a placeholder.

**If the user truly can't answer one** (e.g., they've never gotten a referral), note it as a gap and keep moving. Flag it in Section 7 of the blueprint.

### The 7 Questions

**Q1: "What do you sell, and who is your ideal customer?"**
We need to know the offer and the audience before designing a referral engine around them.

*If stuck, ask:* "In one sentence: I help [type of person] achieve [result]. They typically find me through [channel]."

**Q2: "Who are your happiest customers right now?"**
These are your referral engine. We start here — not with strangers, but with people who already love what you do.

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about customers who've thanked you, left reviews, or told friends. Who are they? Even one or two names count."

**Q3: "When are your customers most delighted — the peak satisfaction moment?"**
This is when to ask for referrals. Timing is everything. Ask too early and they haven't felt the value. Ask too late and the emotion has faded.

*If stuck, ask:* "At what point in working with you does someone say 'wow, this was worth it'? After the first result? After the first week? After a specific milestone?"

**Q4: "Who else serves your ideal customer but ISN'T a competitor?"**
These are potential hosts and partners — businesses with complementary audiences who would benefit from introducing your offer.

*If stuck, ask:* "If your ideal customer has a toolbox of services they use, what else is in that toolbox? Accountant, designer, coach, software provider, community leader?"

**Q5: "Have you ever gotten a referral? How did it happen?"**
Understanding organic referrals reveals the natural mechanism already at work. We want to amplify what's working, not invent something artificial.

*If stuck, ask:* "Has anyone ever sent you a customer? How did it come about? Even one time counts. What did they say, and why did they think of you?"

**Q6: "What would make a referral feel natural and valuable — not forced or awkward?"**
Referral programs fail when they feel transactional. The best referrals happen when someone genuinely wants to share something valuable with someone they care about.

*If stuck, ask:* "If your best customer referred a friend, what would make BOTH the friend AND your customer feel good about it? What would feel generous rather than salesy?"

**Q7: "What incentive (if any) would motivate referrals?"**
Could be monetary, access-based, reciprocal, or nothing at all. Some audiences refer because they want to help friends. Others because it makes them look smart. Others because they get something tangible.

*If stuck, ask:* "Some people refer because they get a discount. Others because they genuinely want to help friends. Others because it makes THEM look smart and connected. What fits your audience?"

## Rules

- NEVER launch a referral program before ensuring customer satisfaction is high — bad products don't deserve referrals, and asking unsatisfied customers to refer actively damages the brand
- Referral asks must make the REFERRER look good, not needy. "Know anyone who needs help?" is needy. "Who else deserves this result?" makes them a hero
- Timing is everything — ask at peak satisfaction, never during onboarding or when the customer is frustrated
- Host-beneficiary deals must be ZERO-RISK for the host — revenue share only, not upfront fees. The host should never feel like they're gambling their reputation
- The Dream 100 approach requires PATIENCE — 12+ touches over months, not a cold pitch on day one. Concentration beats spray-and-pray
- Never offer incentives that attract referrals from people who wouldn't otherwise refer — that creates low-quality leads who churn
- Referral programs should feel generous, not transactional. "We both get $50" feels transactional. "Your friend gets their first month free" feels generous
- Track referral sources rigorously — know who your best referrers are and treat them like VIPs with personal thank-yous and exclusive access
- Community ambassador programs work ONLY when the community delivers genuine value — never build a referral program disguised as a community
- If Readiness Score is below 25, say so directly and recommend fixing the product/service before building a referral engine
- If generate-persona-playbook output exists, tailor referral messaging to persona psychology and communication preferences
- If design-onboarding-sequence output exists, ensure onboarding SETS UP the referral moment by delivering a clear early win
- If extract-testimonial-stories output exists, use testimonials as social proof in partner pitches and referral messaging
- If brand-voice-router is active, apply it to all scripts, templates, and messaging throughout the blueprint

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- **Fix product or service quality** — referrals require genuinely satisfied customers; this skill identifies satisfaction gaps but does not resolve them
- **Build referral tracking software or tools** — provides the strategy and recommends tools, but does not build or configure software
- **Run the partner outreach campaign** — provides the complete sequence, templates, and deal structures, but execution is the user's responsibility
- **Create community platforms or forums** — designs the engagement strategy, not the technology stack
- **Design affiliate programs** — affiliate programs (paying strangers per sale) are structurally different from referral programs (leveraging existing relationships); this skill covers referrals and strategic partnerships only
- **Handle legal or compliance review** — referral incentives may have tax, FTC disclosure, or contractual implications; the user should consult legal counsel for their jurisdiction
- **Replace paid acquisition** — referral engines complement paid channels, they do not eliminate the need for other growth strategies
