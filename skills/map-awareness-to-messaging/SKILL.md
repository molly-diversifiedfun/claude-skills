# Map Awareness to Messaging

## What This Skill Does

Takes your product, audience, and channels — and determines exactly WHERE your audience sits on the awareness spectrum, WHAT messaging strategy matches each level, and WHICH skills to run next. This is the strategy layer that sits above every content and copy skill in the ecosystem.

Getting awareness level wrong is the most expensive mistake in marketing. Showing a sales page to someone who doesn't know they have a problem wastes every dollar and every word. Running educational content to someone who already trusts you and just needs the offer wastes their attention. This skill prevents both.

The output is a complete Awareness & Messaging Map: awareness classifications per channel, market sophistication assessment, messaging strategies per level, a skill router that tells you exactly which skills to run, channel-specific messaging matrices, a pre-suasion plan, and gap analysis with recommendations. Strategy first, content second.

## Phase 1: Discovery Questions

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Help them think through it using their existing knowledge. Most business owners intuitively know their audience's awareness level — they just haven't named it. Use the "If stuck" prompts to guide them.

### The 7 Questions

**Q1: "What do you sell, and what problem does it solve?"**
Not features. The problem and the solution. What pain exists before, and what's true after?

*If stuck, ask:* "In one sentence: 'I help [people] solve [problem] by [method].'"

**Q2: "Describe your ideal customer and where they first encounter you."**
Traffic sources are awareness indicators. Someone clicking a cold Meta ad is in a completely different mental state than someone typing your brand name into Google. The channel reveals the awareness level.

*If stuck, ask:* "How do most people find you? Ads? Search? Referrals? Social media? Speaking?"

**Q3: "What does your ideal customer already KNOW about their problem?"**
This is the core awareness level indicator. Some people don't even know they have a problem. Others have been living with it for years and have tried everything. The gap between those two audiences is enormous.

*If stuck, ask:* "Do they know they have this problem? Have they searched for solutions? Have they seen your competitors? Or are they blissfully unaware that something better exists?"

**Q4: "What does your ideal customer already know about YOUR solution specifically?"**
Awareness of the problem and awareness of YOU are two different things. Someone might know they need help but have never heard of your approach. Someone else might follow you on social media and read every email you send.

*If stuck, ask:* "Have they heard of you before? Do they know what makes you different? Have they visited your site, consumed your content, or been referred by a friend?"

**Q5: "How saturated is your market? How many competitors are saying similar things?"**
This determines market sophistication level — which governs HOW to say your message, not just what to say. A first-to-market product can make direct claims. A product in a crowded market needs a unique mechanism or identity play.

*If stuck, ask:* "When your ideal customer searches for a solution, how many options do they find? Are they all saying the same thing? Has the market heard every claim and promise already?"

**Q6: "What makes your approach or mechanism genuinely different?"**
The unique mechanism, proprietary method, or contrarian angle that separates you from everyone else. This becomes the centerpiece of Solution Aware and Product Aware messaging.

*If stuck, ask:* "If you lined up 5 competitors, what would you say that NONE of them would say? What's the thing you believe that most people in your industry disagree with?"

**Q7: "What are the main entry points to your world? List all channels and touchpoints."**
Website, social media platforms, paid ads, email list, events, referrals, podcast, content, partnerships, speaking — list everything. Each channel may have a DIFFERENT awareness level, which means each channel needs different messaging.

*If stuck, ask:* "List every place someone might first encounter your brand. Include both places you actively promote and places people find you organically."

## Phase 2: Generate the Awareness & Messaging Map

After gathering all 7 answers, generate the complete map across 7 sections. Do not skip sections — each one builds on the previous.

### Section 1: Awareness Level Classification

Classify the awareness level for each audience segment and entry point using Eugene Schwartz's 5 Levels of Market Awareness (Breakthrough Advertising, 1966):

**The 5 Levels:**
- **Level 1 — Completely Unaware:** Don't know they have a problem. Strategy: Lead with story or shocking claim.
- **Level 2 — Problem Aware:** Feel the pain but don't know solutions exist. Strategy: Agitate the problem, then introduce solution category.
- **Level 3 — Solution Aware:** Know solutions exist but not YOUR product. Strategy: Differentiate your mechanism/approach.
- **Level 4 — Product Aware:** Know your product but haven't bought. Strategy: Overcome objections, provide proof, add incentives.
- **Level 5 — Most Aware:** Know and trust you. Strategy: Just make the offer.

### Section 2: Market Sophistication Assessment

Where is the market on Schwartz's 5 Levels of Market Sophistication?

**The 5 Levels:**
- **Level 1 — First to Market:** Be direct. "This product does X."
- **Level 2 — Second Wave:** Enlarge the claim. "Faster/bigger/better."
- **Level 3 — Mechanism Stage:** Introduce unique mechanism. "Through [proprietary method]."
- **Level 4 — Enlarged Mechanism:** Make mechanism more elaborate/refined.
- **Level 5 — Prospect Identification:** Market so saturated, win through identity. "For people who ___."

### Section 3: Messaging Strategy Per Awareness Level

For each awareness level present in the audience (from Section 1), generate a complete messaging strategy block:

**Level [X]: [Name]**
- **Opening strategy:** How to start the conversation at this level
- **Content type:** What format works (story, education, comparison, proof, offer)
- **Key message:** The ONE thing to communicate at this level
- **Tone:** How to sound (educational, empathetic, authoritative, direct)
- **CTA type:** What to ask them to do next (follow, read, watch, download, buy)
- **Pre-suasion move:** What to prime BEFORE the message lands (from Cialdini's Pre-Suasion framework — redirect attention, create associations, leverage identity)
- **Example headline:** A headline that would work at this level for this specific business
- **Skill to use:** Which skill produces content for this level

Generate one block for each level that appears in the Section 1 table. Most businesses will have 2-3 levels to address.

### Section 6: The Pre-Suasion Plan

Based on Robert Cialdini's Pre-Suasion framework (2016) — optimal persuasion requires optimal pre-suasion. The moment BEFORE your message matters as much as the message itself.

For each key channel or touchpoint:

- **What to prime BEFORE the main message:** The identity, emotion, or association to activate (e.g., prime competence before showing a gap, prime belonging before offering community)
- **The privileged moment:** When attention is most receptive and the audience is most open to influence (e.g., right after a frustration, during a transition, at the start of a new quarter)
- **The association to create:** Link your message to something the audience already values (e.g., link your method to their professional identity, link your offer to something they already trust)
- **The identity trigger:** "As someone who [values/believes/does X]..." — frame the message so accepting it is consistent with who they already see themselves as

### Section 7: Messaging Gaps & Recommendations

Analyze the complete map for mismatches, gaps, and opportunities:

- **Missing awareness levels:** Are there levels the user isn't addressing? If all messaging targets Level 4-5 but most traffic is Level 1-2, that's the biggest gap.
- **Channel-message mismatch:** Are they using solution-aware messaging on unaware audiences? Product-aware pitches on cold traffic? This is the most common and most expensive mistake.
- **Sophistication mismatch:** Are they still making basic Level 1 claims in a Level 4-5 market? Are competitors all saying the same thing while the user sounds identical?
- **Pre-suasion gaps:** Are they jumping straight to the pitch without priming? Is there no warm-up before the ask?
- **Recommended messaging sequence:** The optimal order of skills to run based on this analysis. Prioritize by impact — which awareness level, if addressed correctly, would move the most revenue?

## Rules

- **The MOST COMMON mistake is using the wrong awareness level.** Showing a sales page to someone who doesn't know they have a problem is the single biggest waste of marketing spend. Flag this aggressively when you see it.
- **Most cold traffic (ads) is Level 1-2. Most warm traffic (email list, referrals) is Level 4-5.** Treat them differently. A single messaging strategy across all channels is almost always wrong.
- **Market sophistication determines HOW to say it. Market awareness determines WHAT to say.** Both matter. A Level 3 sophistication market needs mechanisms. A Level 5 sophistication market needs identity. Get both right.
- **In Level 5 sophistication markets (most online markets in 2025-2026), identity-based messaging wins over claims and mechanisms.** If the market has heard every claim and seen every mechanism, the only differentiator is WHO the message is for.
- **Pre-suasion is not manipulation — it's ensuring the audience is in the right mental state to receive your message.** Priming competence before showing a gap is ethical. Priming fear to create false urgency is not.
- **Never recommend "just make the offer" unless the audience is truly Level 5 (Most Aware).** Most businesses overestimate how aware their audience is. When in doubt, assume one level lower.
- **Each channel may have a DIFFERENT awareness level.** A single "messaging strategy" applied uniformly across all channels is a recipe for wasted spend. Map each channel individually.
- **This skill produces STRATEGY, not content.** It tells you WHAT to say and WHERE to say it. It routes to other skills for HOW to say it. Do not write ads, emails, or sales pages in this skill.
- **If generate-persona-playbook output exists, align awareness levels to persona segments.** Different personas within the same audience may sit at different awareness levels.
- **If brand-voice-router is active, note how voice should shift across awareness levels.** Voice often needs to be more educational and empathetic at Level 1-2, more authoritative and direct at Level 4-5.
- **Don't over-complicate.** Most businesses have 2-3 awareness levels to address, not all 5. If the analysis shows all 5, double-check — it's more likely that the audience is concentrated in 2-3 levels with edge cases at the extremes.

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- Write the actual content (routes to the right skill for that — see Section 4)
- Create ads, emails, sales pages, or any finished copy (provides the strategy those are built on)
- Conduct market research, surveys, or competitive analysis
- Design the visual brand, creative assets, or landing pages
- Build funnels, automations, or tech infrastructure
- Replace business strategy, positioning, or product-market fit work
- Generate persona profiles (use generate-persona-playbook for that — this skill consumes persona data, it doesn't create it)
