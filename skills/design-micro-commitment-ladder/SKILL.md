# Design Micro-Commitment Ladder

## What This Skill Does

Takes your highest-ticket offer and reverse-engineers the entire psychological journey that turns a stranger into a premium buyer. Each rung on the ladder is a progressively larger commitment — a small "yes" that makes the next "yes" feel natural. The architecture is grounded in Commitment & Consistency (Cialdini), the Foot-in-the-Door effect (Freedman & Fraser, 1966), and Russell Brunson's Value Ladder framework.

The most critical transition in any funnel is the moment a lead becomes a buyer. A $7 purchase transforms someone's self-identity from "browser" to "customer" — and buyers are 10-15x more likely to buy again than non-buyers. This skill designs that identity threshold (the tripwire), the self-liquidating math that covers your ad costs, and every step above and below it so nobody falls through the cracks between free and premium.

This is commitment architecture, not sales copy or funnel page design. You get the full ladder structure, tripwire economics, psychological mapping at each transition, gap analysis against what you already have, and conversion path planning. Building the actual pages, emails, and checkout flows is downstream work.

## Phase 1: Commitment Journey Discovery

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before moving to the next.

**If the user is stuck on any question:** Help them think through it using the specific "If stuck" prompt below. Reference common patterns for their business type and map out what they already have, even informally.

### The 7 Questions

**Q1: "What's your highest-ticket offer — the most someone can pay you?"**
We design backward from the top. The ladder exists to move people UP to this offer, so we need to know where "up" is. Include the price, what it delivers, how it's delivered, and who it's for.

*If stuck, ask:* "What's the most expensive thing you sell or want to sell? If you don't have one yet, what's the dream offer — the thing you'd sell if the right person walked through the door with money in hand?"

**Q2: "What's the lowest-friction way someone can first engage with you?"**
Free content, lead magnet, quiz, free consultation, community, social media — what's the very first touchpoint? We need to know where the ladder starts.

*If stuck, ask:* "If a stranger discovered you today, what could they do that takes less than 2 minutes and zero money? Download something? Watch something? Take a quiz? Join a group?"

**Q3: "What are the natural stepping stones between free and premium?"**
Think about intermediate offers. What could someone buy for $7-$27? For $97-$297? For $997+? These don't need to exist yet — we're mapping the ideal path.

*If stuck, ask:* "Think about it this way — what's the smallest problem you could solve for someone? What's the medium problem? What's the biggest? Each of those becomes a rung on the ladder."

**Q4: "What does each step PROVE to the buyer?"**
Each commitment should reduce a specific risk or eliminate a specific doubt. The $7 purchase proves something that makes the $97 purchase feel safer. The $97 purchase proves something that makes the $997 purchase feel obvious.

*If stuck, ask:* "When someone buys your $7 thing, what do they learn about you that makes them consider the $97 thing? What doubt gets eliminated? What shifts from 'maybe' to 'definitely'?"

**Q5: "What's your current customer acquisition cost and process?"**
How do you get customers today? What does it cost per customer? Do you run ads? Use organic content? Referrals? We need this to design the self-liquidating math.

*If stuck, ask:* "How do people find you and buy from you today? Roughly how much does it cost you (in money or time) to get one paying customer? If you run ads, what do you spend per lead?"

**Q6: "Do you have existing products at different price points?"**
List everything you currently sell or give away, from cheapest to most expensive. Include free lead magnets, low-ticket offers, courses, services, premium packages — all of it. We'll map what exists and find the gaps.

*If stuck, ask:* "Walk me through everything someone can get from you, starting with free stuff and going up. Include anything you've retired or paused — it might fit the ladder."

**Q7: "What would make your ideal customer's first purchase feel like a no-brainer?"**
The tripwire needs to feel so valuable relative to its price that NOT buying feels silly. What could you offer for $7-$27 that delivers 5-10x its price in value?

*If stuck, ask:* "What could you sell for $7-$27 that someone would say 'that's worth 10x what I paid'? Think templates, checklists, mini-trainings, toolkits, swipe files — something quick to consume and immediately useful."

## Phase 2: Generate the Micro-Commitment Ladder

After gathering all 7 answers, generate a structured document with these 7 sections.

### Section 1: The Complete Ladder

Visual representation of the full commitment journey. Map every step from first touch to highest-ticket offer:

```
STEP 0: [Free Content] — $0
  Purpose: [specific purpose]
  Format: [what it is]
  ↓ (psychological shift: curiosity → interest)

STEP 1: [Lead Magnet] — $0 (email exchange)
  Purpose: [specific purpose]
  Format: [what it is]
  ↓ (psychological shift: interest → engagement)

STEP 2: [Tripwire] — $[X]
  Purpose: Transform lead → buyer (identity threshold)
  Format: [what it is]
  ↓ (psychological shift: observer → investor)

STEP 3: [Core Offer] — $[XXX]
  Purpose: [specific purpose]
  Format: [what it is]
  ↓ (psychological shift: customer → committed)

STEP 4: [Profit Maximizer] — $[X,XXX]
  Purpose: [specific purpose]
  Format: [what it is]
  ↓ (psychological shift: committed → enrolled)

STEP 5: [High-Ticket] — $[XX,XXX]
  Purpose: [specific purpose]
  Format: [what it is]
  (psychological shift: enrolled → partner)
```

Adjust the number of steps based on the user's answers. Some ladders need 4 steps, others need 7. Never leave a gap larger than 10x between adjacent price points — insert an intermediate rung if needed.

### Section 2: Tripwire / Self-Liquidating Offer Design

The most critical step — turning leads into buyers. This is where identity transformation happens:

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Product** | [what it is — specific to their business] |
| **Price** | $[X] |
| **Perceived Value** | $[Y] (minimum 5x price) |
| **Format** | [digital product, mini-course, template, toolkit, etc.] |
| **Delivery** | [instant download, email drip, member area, etc.] |
| **Time to Consume** | [minutes, not hours — fast wins build momentum] |
| **What It Proves** | [what buying this demonstrates about the seller's quality/expertise] |
| **Natural Next Step** | [what the buyer will want after consuming this] |

Then design the checkout maximizers:

**Order Bump** ($[X] — natural complement to tripwire):
- What it is: [description]
- Why it works at checkout: [reason it feels like "add fries"]

**One-Click Upsell #1** ($[XX] — offered immediately after purchase):
- What it is: [description]
- Why it works post-purchase: [leverages buying momentum, wallet already open]

**One-Click Upsell #2** ($[XXX] — optional, offered after upsell #1):
- What it is: [description]
- Why it works here: [reason]

### Section 3: Self-Liquidating Math

Does the front-end revenue cover customer acquisition costs? Use conservative estimates:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Tripwire price | $[X] |
| Order bump ($[X] x [Y]% take rate) | +$[X] |
| Upsell 1 ($[XX] x [Y]% take rate) | +$[X] |
| Upsell 2 ($[XXX] x [Y]% take rate) | +$[X] |
| **Expected revenue per buyer** | **$[X]** |
| Cost per lead (estimated) | $[X] |
| Opt-in to buyer conversion (estimated) | [X]% |
| **Cost per buyer** | **$[X]** |
| **SLO surplus/deficit** | **+/-$[X]** |

Use these conservative benchmarks unless the user provides actuals:
- Opt-in to tripwire buyer: 5-10%
- Order bump take rate: 10-15%
- Upsell 1 take rate: 5-10%
- Upsell 2 take rate: 3-7%

If the math shows a deficit, provide specific recommendations:
- Raise tripwire price
- Add or improve order bump
- Lower ad costs through better targeting
- Improve landing page conversion
- Accept the deficit if back-end LTV justifies it (show the math)

### Section 4: Commitment Psychology Map

For each step in the ladder, map the psychological mechanics:

| Step | The Ask | Risk Perceived by Buyer | Actual Risk | Psychological Principle | What Removes Hesitation |
|------|---------|------------------------|-------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Free Content | Attention | Time waste | Very low | Curiosity gap | Headline/hook quality |
| Lead Magnet | Give email | Spam, time waste | Very low | Reciprocity | High perceived value of freebie |
| Tripwire | Spend $[X] | "Is this legit?" | Negligible | Foot-in-the-door | Absurd value-to-price ratio |
| Core Offer | Spend $[XXX] | "Will this work for ME?" | Moderate | Commitment & Consistency + Social Proof | Testimonials, guarantee, tripwire experience |
| Profit Maximizer | Spend $[X,XXX] | "Is this worth the investment?" | Significant | Authority + Sunk Cost | ROI math, case studies, prior results |
| High-Ticket | Spend $[XX,XXX] | "Am I making a mistake?" | Major | Unity + Identity | Application process, personal connection, proof of transformation |

Customize every cell based on the user's specific business, audience, and offers.

### Section 6: Conversion Path Mapping

For each transition in the ladder, specify how people move from one step to the next:

| From → To | Trigger Event | Content/Mechanism | Timing | Expected Conversion Rate |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|--------|------------------------|
| Stranger → Free Content | [discovery channel] | [SEO, social, ads, referral] | Ongoing | Varies by channel |
| Free Content → Lead Magnet | [what triggers opt-in] | [CTA, content upgrade, pop-up] | Immediate | 20-40% of engaged visitors |
| Lead Magnet → Tripwire | [what triggers first purchase] | [thank-you page offer, email sequence] | 0-7 days | 5-15% of leads |
| Tripwire → Core Offer | [what triggers upgrade] | [email nurture, proof of results, webinar] | 7-30 days | 10-20% of tripwire buyers |
| Core → Profit Maximizer | [what triggers expansion] | [results-based outreach, case studies] | 30-90 days | 5-10% of core buyers |
| Profit Maximizer → High-Ticket | [what triggers premium commitment] | [application, call, relationship] | 60-180 days | 3-8% of maximizer buyers |

Include specific email/content strategies for each transition, not just generic descriptions.

### Section 7: Ladder Health Check & Recommendations

Evaluate the complete ladder against these criteria:

- **Price jumps**: Flag any step where the price increase exceeds 10x. Recommend an intermediate offer to bridge the gap.
- **Value continuity**: Does each step naturally create desire for the next? Or does the ladder feel disconnected?
- **Proof accumulation**: Does each step provide evidence that makes the next step feel safer? Identify proof gaps.
- **Missing rungs**: Which steps in the ladder are empty? Prioritize by revenue impact.
- **Cannibalization risk**: Do any steps compete with each other? Does the tripwire give away too much, reducing core offer demand?
- **Self-liquidation viability**: Can the front-end cover ad costs? If not, what needs to change?
- **Identity transformation**: Does the tripwire actually shift someone from "lead" to "buyer"? Is it a real product or just a glorified lead magnet?
- **Time-to-value**: Can people consume and benefit from each step quickly enough to maintain momentum?

Provide a prioritized action plan: what to build first, second, third — with rationale for the sequencing.

## Rules

- Design BACKWARD from the highest-ticket offer — the ladder exists to move people UP, not to sell trinkets at the bottom.
- The tripwire is NOT about profit — it is about identity transformation. A lead who spends $7 becomes a buyer. That psychological shift is worth more than the $7.
- Each step must solve a COMPLETE (if small) problem — not a teaser that only works with the next step. Every rung delivers standalone value.
- Price jumps must not exceed 10x between adjacent steps — a $7 to $997 jump loses people. Insert intermediate rungs to bridge gaps.
- The order bump must be a natural complement (not a different product) — "add fries?" not "also buy a car."
- One-click upsells work because the wallet is already open — use this moment wisely. The upsell should accelerate or deepen the tripwire result.
- Self-liquidating math must use REALISTIC conversion estimates — 5-10% opt-in to buyer, 10-15% order bump take rate, 5-10% upsell take rate. Optimistic math helps nobody.
- Never manufacture fake scarcity or urgency on tripwire offers — the low price IS the urgency.
- Each step must answer: "What doubt does this eliminate?" — proof accumulates through the ladder, reducing perceived risk at every transition.
- If design-offer-ladder output exists, align the commitment ladder with the offer ladder tiers. The two should be complementary, not contradictory.
- If build-irresistible-offer output exists, use the value stack for core and premium offers rather than inventing new ones.
- If generate-persona-playbook output exists, map personas to where they enter the ladder and what path they take through it.
- If design-pricing-architecture output exists, use validated price points rather than guessing.
- Apply brand-voice-router if active — all ladder descriptions and transition language should match the brand voice.

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- **Write sales copy** for each step (use build-conversion-sales-letter)
- **Build funnel pages** or landing pages (use funnel-landing-page-designer)
- **Create the products themselves** — this designs the architecture, not the content
- **Set up payment processing**, checkout flows, or tech stack
- **Run ads or drive traffic** — this designs what happens after someone arrives
- **Design email sequences** between steps (use build-email-story-engine + create-tag-based-funnel-system)
- **Validate pricing** through market research (use design-pricing-architecture for that)
