# Five Questioning Channels

Each channel bypasses the user's rehearsed verbal narrative through a different cognitive route. The skill interleaves all five based on what the user's responses reveal — it never uses just one.

---

## 1. Socratic Elenchus — Forcing Beliefs Into the Same Room

**Purpose:** Extract a confident thesis, surface additional beliefs the person already holds, let them discover that those beliefs contradict the original thesis. The contradiction emerges from WITHIN their own belief set.

**When to use:** The user states a confident, unexamined belief. There's enough complexity in the topic to contain hidden contradictions.

**When it breaks:** Triggers ego-defense. Shame produces fight-or-flight, not curiosity. Never escalate tone — let the contradiction do the work.

**Signature move:** Surface implications the user hasn't considered, then ask if they hold all those implications.

### Example Questions

- "You believe [X] — what would have to be true for [X] to hold?"
- "Do you also believe all of those things?"
- "If [X] is really true, what follows from that?"
- "Are you comfortable with ALL the implications of that?"
- "What's the strongest argument against what you just said?"
- "You said [X] — does that apply in [edge case Y] too?"

### How It Works in Practice

User says: "I should always prioritize the team's needs over my own."

Elenchus sequence:
1. "What would have to be true for that to hold in every case?"
2. (They list conditions) "Do you believe all of those conditions are met right now?"
3. "If you always put the team first, what happens when your judgment is better than the team's consensus?"

The user discovers their own contradiction. You never point it out.

## 2. Clean Language — Zero-Contamination Questions

**Purpose:** Use ONLY the person's exact words to explore their thinking. Prevents the interviewer from injecting frameworks, assumptions, or interpretations. Treats metaphors as cognitive infrastructure, not decoration.

**When to use:** The user is vague, emotional, or uses metaphorical language. Any time a word seems to carry more weight than its dictionary definition.

**When it breaks:** If used carelessly on negative emotional states — Clean questions DEEPEN whatever they target. Don't deepen "hopeless" without purpose. Also breaks if you add interpretation.

**Signature move:** Mirror their exact word back in a question structure.

### Core Question Patterns

These two account for roughly 50% of all Clean Language questions:

- **"What kind of [their word] is that [their word]?"** — asks for attributes
- **"Is there anything else about [their word]?"** — opens for more content

Additional patterns:

- **"And that's [their word] like what?"** — invites metaphor
- **"And where is [their word]?"** — spatial/embodied location
- **"And when [their word], what happens next?"** — temporal sequence
- **"And what happens just before [their word]?"** — causal chain

### How It Works in Practice

User says: "I feel stuck."

Clean Language sequence:
1. "What kind of stuck is that stuck?"
2. (They say "Like I'm spinning my wheels") "And when you're spinning your wheels, what kind of spinning is that?"
3. "Is there anything else about that spinning?"

**Critical rule:** Use their EXACT words. Not synonyms. Not paraphrases. If they said "stuck," you say "stuck" — not "blocked," not "stalled," not "trapped." Word substitution contaminates the exploration.

## 3. MI Directional Pull — Structurally Pulling for Reasons

**Purpose:** Based on Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick). The balance of change talk vs. sustain talk is the strongest predictor of behavior change. These questions are engineered to elicit desire, ability, reasons, and need — pulling the user to hear themselves articulate their own position.

**When to use:** The user is stuck between options. Ambivalent. Saying "I should" but not moving. Going back and forth.

**When it breaks:** User has already decided (no ambivalence to work with). Or the barriers are structural/external rather than motivational.

**Signature move:** The importance ruler — ask for a number, then ask why it isn't lower.

### Example Questions

- "On a scale of 0-10, how important is this to you?" → "You said 6, not 1 — why not lower?"
- "What matters most to you about this?"
- "If you made this change, how would things be different a year from now?"
- "What's the best reason to do this?"
- "You keep coming back to [X] — what is it about [X]?"
- "What would you lose if you didn't do this?"

### How It Works in Practice

User says: "I should probably leave this job but I keep not doing it."

MI sequence:
1. "How important is leaving, 0 to 10?"
2. (They say "7") "You said 7, not 2 — why not lower?"
3. (They give reasons) "And if you did leave, what changes a year from now?"

The user hears themselves articulate their own reasons. Self-perception principle: hearing yourself say it strengthens the commitment (or reveals it's not actually there).

## 4. Sensory Specificity — Bypassing the Rehearsed Narrative

**Purpose:** Asking about a specific sensory moment forces episodic memory retrieval. This activates sensory and emotional processing rather than the verbal narrative track the user has rehearsed. Drawn from longform journalism interviewing (Cal Fussman, Terry Gross, Studs Terkel).

**When to use:** The user gives a polished, rehearsed-sounding answer. They're telling you their "story about" the thing rather than the thing itself. You hear prepared talking points rather than live thinking.

**When it breaks:** If there's no real experience to draw from (purely hypothetical topics). Or if used before rapport exists — feels invasive.

**Signature move:** Drop from abstract to concrete moment.

### Example Questions

- "What's the first moment you knew?"
- "You said [X] — what do you mean by that? What were you seeing?"
- "What did you see when you walked in?"
- "When was the last time you felt [their word]? What was happening?"
- "Take me to the moment you decided. Where were you?"
- "What did they actually say — the exact words?"

### How It Works in Practice

User says: "I've always been passionate about building products."

Sensory specificity sequence:
1. "When was the first time you realized that? What were you doing?"
2. (They describe a specific moment) "What happened next?"
3. "What was it about THAT moment specifically?"

The specific memory reveals what the abstract narrative conceals. "Passionate about building products" might actually be "I like the control" or "I hate depending on other people's decisions" — the real insight lives in the specific memory, not the polished story.

---

## 5. Contrast Questions — Holding Two Statements Side by Side

**Purpose:** Place two things the user has said next to each other and let the tension speak for itself. This is the modern form of the Socratic elenchus — the contradiction comes from the user's own statements across the conversation.

**When to use:** The user has said two things that don't obviously fit together. One earlier in the conversation, one later. Or their stated priorities don't match their described behavior.

**When it breaks:** If the "contradiction" is actually nuance you're flattening. Make sure both statements were clear and confident, not exploratory.

**Signature move:** Hold both statements without judgment, ask how they fit.

### Example Questions

- "You said [X] matters most to you, but you also said you've been spending all your time on [Y]. What's happening there?"
- "Earlier you said [X]. Just now you said [Y]. How do those fit together for you?"
- "I notice [X] and [Y] sitting next to each other — how do you see those fitting together?"
- "You described wanting [X] but choosing [Y] every time. What's driving that?"

### How It Works in Practice

User said (question 2): "Family time is my top priority."
User said (question 5): "I've been working 70-hour weeks for the last year."

Contrast question:
> "You said family time is your top priority, but you've been working 70-hour weeks. What's happening there?"

**Critical rules:**
- Never say "you contradicted yourself." The question is neutral.
- Use their exact words for both halves.
- Ask "what's happening there?" not "why?" — "why" triggers justification. "What's happening" triggers observation.
- Let the user sit with the tension. Don't fill the silence.

## Channel Selection Quick Reference

| What you're hearing | Primary channel | Backup channel |
|---------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Confident assertion, untested | Socratic elenchus | Contrast (if you have a second statement) |
| Vague, metaphorical, emotional words | Clean Language | Sensory specificity |
| "I should but..." / ambivalence | MI directional pull | Contrast |
| Polished, rehearsed-sounding | Sensory specificity | Clean Language |
| Two statements that don't fit | Contrast | Socratic elenchus |
| "I don't know" with engagement | Clean Language | Sensory specificity |
| "I don't know" with flatness | Shift thread entirely | Different topic angle |
