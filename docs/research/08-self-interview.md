# Self-Interview — Research Results

Five mechanically distinct questioning techniques each bypass the rehearsed narrative through a genuinely different channel: Socratic elenchus (forces contradictions within your own belief set), Clean Language (uses only your exact words to avoid contamination), Motivational Interviewing (structurally pulls specific kinds of self-talk), journalist sensory-specificity (bypasses abstractions through episodic memory retrieval), and Gendlin's Focusing (body → words, not words → body). The strongest insight: every effective technique works by **redirecting attention away from the person's rehearsed verbal narrative** and toward a channel they haven't pre-processed.

---

## Q1: Five questioning techniques that surface unarticulated thinking

### 1. Socratic elenchus — forcing beliefs into the same room

Extracts a confident thesis, surfaces additional beliefs the person already holds, demonstrates they contradict the original thesis. The contradiction emerges from *within the person's own belief set*. Produces aporia — genuine perplexity — the precondition for real thinking.

**Questions:** "You believe X — what would have to be true for X to hold? Do you also believe all of those things?" / "If X is really true, what follows — are you comfortable with *all* the implications?"

**Works when:** confident but unexamined beliefs with enough complexity to contain hidden contradictions. **Breaks when:** triggers ego-defense (shame produces fight-or-flight, not curiosity).

### 2. Clean Language — questions that carry zero contamination

David Grove's 12 questions built from the person's exact words. Two questions — "What kind of X is that X?" and "Is there anything else about X?" — account for ~50% of all questions. Treats metaphors as cognitive infrastructure, not decoration. People use ~6 metaphors per minute.

**Questions:** "And what kind of [their word] is that [their word]?" / "And that's [their word] like what?"

**Works when:** exploring complex, emotional, or vaguely felt territory. **Breaks when:** facilitator adds interpretation, or used carelessly on negative states (Clean questions *deepen whatever they target*).

### 3. MI open questions — structurally pulling specific kinds of talk

The balance of change talk vs. sustain talk is the strongest predictor of behavior change (Miller & Rollnick). Questions engineered to elicit desire/ability/reasons/need. Self-perception principle: when people hear themselves articulate reasons, commitment strengthens.

**Questions:** "On a scale of 0-10, how important is this? ... You said 6, not 1 — why not lower?" / "If you made this change, how would life be different a year from now?"

**Works when:** ambivalence. **Breaks when:** already decided, or structural barriers rather than motivational ones.

### 4. Journalist sensory-specificity — bypassing rehearsed narrative

Asking about a specific sensory moment forces episodic memory retrieval, activating sensory and emotional processing rather than the verbal narrative track. Cal Fussman's first question to Gorbachev bypassed decades of rehearsed geopolitical answers.

**Questions:** "What's the first moment you knew?" / "You said [X] — what do you mean by that?" / "What did you see when you walked in?"

**Works when:** deep preparation and longform rhythm. **Breaks when:** trained media deflector, or interviewer challenges before rapport exists.

### 5. Gendlin's Focusing — the body as information channel

Body → words, not words → body. 15 years of University of Chicago research: the single factor distinguishing successful therapy clients was turning attention to a vague bodily sensation and staying with it until it clarified. Hallmark: the "felt shift" — physical release when conscious mind receives what the body was signaling.

**Questions:** "What wants my awareness right now?" / "What is it about this whole problem that makes it feel so [heavy/stuck/tight]?"

**Works when:** basic body awareness and tolerance for vagueness. **Breaks when:** highly intellectualized person can't shift from head to body.

**Confidence: High.** Each methodology has decades of practice, distinct cognitive targets.

## Q2: Progression and stopping signals

**Sequencing:** Default funnel (broad → specific). Switch to inverted funnel (specific → broad) if user gives very short or "I don't know" responses early.

**"I don't know" handling:** Distinguish sustain talk (productive resistance about the topic) from discord (dissonance with the process). One probe: "What comes to mind even if it seems incomplete?" If engagement: stay. If flatness: move. **Never push past two consecutive "I don't know" responses on the same thread.**

**Thread commitment:** Stay when new facets emerge, emotional deepening occurs, surprise appears. Shift when responses become repetitive, circular, or increasingly brief. Minimum: 3 questions per thread before considering a shift.

**Clarity signals (Gendlin + neuroscience):** Suddenness, new content generation, surprise ("Oh!"), bodily shift. Insight-derived solutions correct **94%** of the time vs. 78% for analytical solutions (Salvi et al.). Premature closure: intellectual summary without emotional coloring, no new content, quick agreement to end.

**Session length:** **15-25 minutes, 8-12 substantive questions.** Critical: reflection-rumination boundary — self-analysis focused on gaining new insights → positive; repetitive negative dwelling → negative. Tool must produce insight, not rumination.

**Confidence: High** for sequencing and clarity signals. **Medium** for question count.

## Q3: Output artifact

**The clarity map** is the most aligned format: foregrounds *where thinking is solid versus where it's still fuzzy*. No existing tool produces this.

Narrative therapy's externalization (White & Epston, 1990) supports creating a separate artifact: makes internal states tangible, creates distance for new perspective. 2025 *Frontiers in Psychiatry* paper found GenAI can serve as "artificial third" creating "Winnicottian playful space."

**Key finding from journaling apps (Reflection.app, Rosebud):** Most valued outputs are AI-synthesized patterns across sessions over time, not single-session artifacts.

**Confidence: Medium.** Clarity map theoretically supported but empirically untested.

## Q4: Existing tools and failure modes

**Universal failure:** Sycophancy — AI defaults to validation. Nick Bolton (Animas Coaching): "My 'simple question' suddenly became 'profound,' my half-baked plan was 'mythic'... If everything is always good, then nothing is truly good."

**Five failure patterns across all tools:**
1. Sycophancy / false validation
2. Repetitive questioning loops
3. Loss of conversational thread
4. Surface-level probing that doesn't reach uncomfortable territory
5. False confidence from polished output (fluent text triggers trust bias)

**Rubber duck debugging** works through forced linearization (speaking at 120-180 wpm vs. thinking at 350-500 forces gaps to become visible) but breaks when the person genuinely doesn't know the answer.

**Confidence: High** for failure mode identification.

## Key Design Decisions

> **Challenge intensity:** Start gentle, escalate. The elenchus shows shame triggers fight-or-flight. Challenge should emerge from the *user's own contradictions*, not from Claude's tone.

> **Exact words vs. paraphrase:** Exact words for key terms carrying emotional weight. Natural language for structural/transitional questions. Grove's contamination research is too strong to ignore.

> **Output artifact:** Clarity map distinguishing solid from fuzzy areas, with explicit caveat that fuzzy areas are findings, not failures.

> **Session length:** Soft cap at 10-12 questions with check-in at question 7-8. Uncapped self-analysis correlates with worse outcomes. Let user override once with awareness.

> **"I don't know" handling:** One probe, then read the response. Engagement = stay. Flatness = move. Never push past two consecutive "I don't know."

> **Whether Claude names observations:** Questions that let the user discover contradictions (Option B). Exception: if user explicitly asks Claude to reflect back. The elenchus works because the person discovers their own contradiction.

## Question Architecture

**Diamond shape:** narrow opening, broadening middle, narrow close.

**Opening (1-2 questions): Thesis extraction.** "What's the one thing you're most trying to figure out?" or "State your current position on X in one sentence." Creates a concrete target.

**Expansion (3-6 questions): Pressure testing through multiple channels.** Interleave techniques based on what emerges: confident belief → elenchus implications, vague → Clean Language, stuck between options → MI directional pull, rehearsed-sounding → sensory specificity. When a question produces surprise, stay on that thread. When it produces repetition, shift channels.

**Pressure point (questions 5-8): The turn.** Contrast question holding two things the user said side by side: "You said X matters most, but you also said you've been doing Y. What's happening there?" The elenchus in modern form — contradiction from the user's own statements.

**Resolution (2-3 questions): Crystallization and gap-marking.** Do not summarize. Ask the user: "What's clearer now than when we started?" then "What's still fuzzy?" Final question: "What's the next thing you'd need to figure out?" Positions unresolved questions as valuable output, not failure.

**Governing principle:** Claude's questions should feel like they're *following* the user's thinking, not leading it. Success = the user says something that surprises *themselves*.
