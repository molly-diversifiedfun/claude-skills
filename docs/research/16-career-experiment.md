# Research: Career Experiment Skill

Synthesized findings for a skill that designs low-stakes, time-bounded career experiments instead of giving career advice. Research conducted May 2026. Target word count for the synthesis: ~1,800.

---

## Q1 [HIGH]: What does experiment-based career exploration look like in practice?

### Findings

Four methodologies dominate the space. They converge on the same insight: people can't think their way to the right career — they have to build something cheap, run it, and read the result.

**Designing Your Life (Burnett & Evans, Stanford d.school).** The most-cited framework. Translates design thinking into life design. Two core moves matter for this skill:

- **Prototype experiences** — small encounters with a possible future that surface assumptions and produce data. Their default prototype is *not* a side project. It's a conversation. Burnett & Evans push readers to "spend time with someone who does the career you're interested in" before any other move. Reasoning: the cost is one coffee, the signal is high, the failure mode is benign.
- **Odyssey Plans** — three different five-year life paths, sketched in parallel. Average answer to "How many lifetimes' worth of living are in you?" was 3.4. The Odyssey exercise breaks the binary "should I or shouldn't I" frame that paralyzes most career questions.
- They explicitly reject reflection-only methods. Their language: "fail forward, fail fast, sneak up on the future." Action precedes clarity, not the other way around.

**Lean Startup applied to careers.** Direct port of Eric Ries's Build-Measure-Learn loop. The career version (MovingWorlds, Roberto Seif's "90-Day Career Experiment," Wharton Executive Education): treat your career hypothesis like a business hypothesis. State the assumption ("I'd love managing people"), build the cheapest possible test ("lead one cross-functional project for 6 weeks"), measure the signal ("did I find 1:1s draining or energizing?"), then keep, iterate, or pivot. Validation comes from concrete actions someone takes, not from imagining what they'd take.

**Real career-pivoter case studies.** Two examples that recur across the literature:
- *Ina Garten* hosted home-cooked dinners for friends for years before leaving her White House budget-analyst job to become the Barefoot Contessa. Each dinner was a free, low-risk test of whether she liked cooking-as-job, not cooking-as-hobby.
- *Will Brown* (economist → farmer) spent weekends learning to care for cows on a working farm before making the switch. He didn't quit, take a course, or get certified first. He volunteered until he had enough reps to know.

The pattern across cases: every successful transition is "an immeasurable number of often tiny, even imperceptible steps forward" — never a single brave leap.

**Dee McCrorey's Micro-Experiments (Pink Slip Pivot toolkit, free).** Built specifically for people navigating layoffs and AI-era role disruption. Frame: "small moves to test direction without commitment." Her positioning is sharp — micro-experiments aren't just exploration, they're *protective* signaling to current employers ("I'm evolving, I'm learning, I'm creating value"). She also ships a Claude Skill version of this approach as part of her toolkit, which is direct prior art for what we're building.

### Sources
- Burnett & Evans, *Designing Your Life* (book + Stanford Life Design Lab)
- Roberto Seif, "The 90-day career experiment," Substack
- MovingWorlds blog, "Use the Lean Startup Method to Find Your Dream Job"
- Wharton Executive Education, "Surviving a Career Change: A Pivot How-To" (Nov 2023)
- *Nature* careers, "Before making a career move, try an experiment" (2025)
- Dee McCrorey, Pink Slip Pivot toolkit (pinkslippivot.com)
- Happen To Your Career podcast — "Designing Career Experiments" episodes

### Confidence: High.
Strong convergence across four independent methodologies. The "test before you leap" frame is borderline consensus.

## Q2 [HIGH]: What experiments can you actually run inside a current role?

### Findings

A taxonomy of seven experiment types. Each rated on three axes: **effort** (hours/week), **signal quality** (how trustworthy the data is), and **risk to current role** (will my boss notice or care).

| # | Experiment | Effort | Signal | Risk | Typical timeline |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Informational interview** (3 conversations w/ people doing the role) | 2-3 hrs total | Medium | None | 1-2 weeks |
| 2 | **Shadow day** (half-day to full-day with someone in the role) | 4-8 hrs | High — surfaces day-to-day reality | Low | 1-2 weeks to schedule |
| 3 | **Stretch assignment** (volunteer for cross-functional work in target area) | 5-10 hrs/wk × 4-12 wks | High | Low — usually rewarded | 4-12 weeks |
| 4 | **Adjacent responsibility** (take on a slice of the new role inside current job — e.g., run one 1:1, write one PRD) | 1-3 hrs/wk × 2-4 wks | High | Very low | 2-4 weeks |
| 5 | **Side project in target domain** (build, ship, or sell something that mimics target work) | 5-15 hrs/wk × 4-8 wks | Very high — closest to real | Low if separate from employer | 4-8 weeks |
| 6 | **Write/speak publicly** (1-3 essays, podcasts, or talks on the target field) | 3-8 hrs each | Medium-high — tests interest + audience | Low | 2-4 weeks per piece |
| 7 | **Pro bono / volunteer** (do the target work for free for a nonprofit or friend) | 4-10 hrs over 2-6 wks | High — real stakes, no career risk | None | 2-6 weeks |

**Patterns from the research:**

- *Stretch assignments are the highest-leverage move inside a role.* They build skills, increase visibility, and let you try management/strategy/whatever without title risk. LeadDev and Mind The Product both recommend running at least one cross-functional project before applying for a manager title — it lets you do the job in a "ghost role" so the title becomes paperwork, not a leap.
- *Shadow days disclose the ugly truths job descriptions hide.* Career services literature is consistent: shadow days expose the real pace of work, the ratio of meetings to deep work, and the specific friction points professionals don't put on LinkedIn.
- *Informational interviews work best with structure.* MIT and University at Buffalo both publish question banks. Top categories: day-to-day reality, skills they actually use most, what they wish they'd known, what surprised them, and a "what would you do in my shoes?" close.
- *Side projects and pro bono work produce the highest-quality signal* — but cost the most time. They're closest to "doing the actual job."
- *Writing publicly is underrated as a test.* Multiple Substack case studies show writing in a new field both clarifies your own thinking AND surfaces audience demand — does anyone respond, share, or DM about your topic? Free demand validation.

### Sources
- Wellesley, MSU, MIT, UConn career services pages on job shadowing + informational interviews
- LeadDev, Mind The Product, Medium: IC → manager transition guides
- Glassdoor on stretch assignments
- Substack case studies: Jessica Drapluk (NP → markets), Jada Butler (portfolio career), Carly Ayres (skill reframing)

### Confidence: High.
Each experiment type has multiple independent sources backing it. The taxonomy is field-tested.

## Q3 [MEDIUM]: Common career transitions and their experiment patterns

### Findings

Five transition types. The structure is universal, but the specific experiments differ.

| Transition | Best experiments | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **IC → manager** | (1) Lead one cross-functional project. (2) Run formal 1:1s with mentees for 4-6 weeks. (3) Own a hiring or onboarding loop. | Manager work is invisible until you do it. These tests surface whether 1:1s drain or energize you — the single biggest predictor of fit. |
| **Technical → product** | (1) Write a PRD for a real feature on your team. (2) Run 5 user interviews. (3) Sit in on the PM's planning meetings for a sprint. | PM work is mostly meetings, writing, and judgment calls. Test the volume of context-switching, not the strategy work. |
| **Corporate → startup/freelance** | (1) Take on 1-2 freelance clients on the side. (2) Write 3 sales proposals and see if anyone pays. (3) Talk to 5 people who made the same jump in the last 2 years. | The day-1 question for startup/freelance isn't "can I do the work" — it's "can I find the work and get paid." Test sales, not delivery. |
| **Career change to new field** | (1) Volunteer or pro bono for 4-6 weeks. (2) Take one paid project in the new field while keeping the day job. (3) Publish 2-3 essays/threads in the new domain. | New fields require credibility you don't have. Volunteer + write builds public proof while testing whether you actually like the work. |
| **Employee → founder** | (1) Pre-sell a product before building it (collect 5-10 deposits). (2) Run a paid pilot with 1-3 customers. (3) Track side-hustle revenue for 3 months — does it cover a meaningful slice of expenses, repeatedly? | Founder fit is a demand question, not an effort question. Validate that someone will pay before quitting anything. |

**Universal structure across all five:** start with the cheapest possible conversation (informational interview), graduate to the cheapest possible action (adjacent responsibility or pro bono), then run the closest-to-real test (side project, paid pilot, or stretch assignment). Don't skip rungs.

### Sources
- Wharton Executive Education, *Surviving a Career Change* (2023)
- Mind The Product, "Practical guide to move from IC to manager"
- Filament/Founders Inc., side-hustle-to-founder validation guides
- Roberto Seif's "90-day career experiment" template

### Confidence: Medium-high.
Transitions are well-documented; specific experiment-counts (5 interviews, 3 essays) are heuristic, not rigorously tested.

## Q4 [MEDIUM]: What makes a good experiment vs. a useless one?

### Findings

Five criteria, drawn from scientific method + lean startup + the career-experiment literature converging:

1. **Testable hypothesis.** A single clear claim with an independent variable, dependent variable, and a way to measure. *Good:* "If I run 5 user interviews this month, I'll learn whether I find synthesis work energizing." *Bad:* "I'll think about whether PM is right for me."
2. **Falsifiable.** You must be able to imagine a result that proves the hypothesis wrong. If every outcome confirms what you already believe, it's not an experiment — it's confirmation bias with a deadline.
3. **Time-bounded.** A signal must arrive within a defined window. The literature converges on 2-12 weeks for individual career experiments. Anything longer becomes "a phase of life" instead of a test.
4. **Clear success/failure signals defined in advance.** "See how it feels" doesn't count. Real signals are specific behaviors, energy patterns, or external responses you can name now: *"Success = I'm energized after at least 3 of 5 user interviews. Failure = I'm drained after 4 of 5."*
5. **Reversible / low downside.** If the experiment fails, you keep your job, your reputation, and your savings. The whole point of experiments is to surface information without requiring courage.

**The single sharpest distinction** between an experiment and "thinking about it": an experiment requires another person to do something. A conversation. A response to your essay. A purchase. A meeting on a calendar. If the entire experiment happens inside your own head, it's not an experiment.

### Sources
- St. Jude Research, "A hypothesis can't be right unless it can be proven wrong"
- Karl Popper on falsifiability (via Wikipedia / Explorable.com summaries)
- Eric Ries, *The Lean Startup* — Build-Measure-Learn loop
- *Nature* careers, "Before making a career move, try an experiment"

### Confidence: High.
The criteria are stable across science and product disciplines. Career writers borrow them directly.

## Key Design Decisions (5-7 tradeoffs)

| Decision | Choice | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Output format: experiments vs. plan vs. advice** | Always experiments. Three of them. Period. | Advice produces inaction. Plans produce overwhelm. Three concrete experiments produce one Monday-morning move. The whole reason for this skill is the gap between "career advice" (useless) and "career experiment" (testable). |
| **How many experiments per request** | Default 3, with explicit effort tiering: one low-effort (≤3 hrs total), one medium (~5 hrs/wk × 2 wks), one high-signal (~10+ hrs over 2-4 wks). | Single experiments feel arbitrary. Five+ feel overwhelming. Three covers the user's risk-appetite range and lets them pick. The tiering ensures at least one is doable this week. |
| **Time bound: hard cap or flexible** | Hard cap at 2 weeks for the *first signal*. Total experiment can run longer but must produce something measurable inside 14 days. | Career questions have a half-life. A 90-day experiment that produces no signal until day 89 is indistinguishable from procrastination. The 2-week first-signal rule forces resolution. |
| **Hypothesis required vs. optional** | Required. Every experiment opens with "Hypothesis: [single testable claim]." | The hypothesis is the experiment. Without it, you're just doing tasks. The act of stating the hypothesis often reveals that the user doesn't actually know what they're testing — which is itself useful. |
| **Success/failure signals: vibes or specifics** | Specifics only. Concrete behaviors, energy patterns, or external responses. No "see how it feels." | "How it feels" is unfalsifiable. Specifics are the difference between an experiment and a journal entry. The skill should refuse to produce vague signals and rewrite them. |
| **Personal advice vs. structured output** | Structured output, no advice. The skill produces experiments; the user produces decisions. | Career advice from an LLM is the bottom quartile of useful career advice. Experiments produce data the user trusts because they generated it. Stay in the experiment-design lane. |
| **Intake: deep interview vs. minimal questions** | Minimal — 3 questions max. Current role/context, target direction or open-ended exploration, time/energy available this month. | The longer the intake, the more the user expects polished advice. Short intake signals "I'm here to design experiments, not to coach you." |
| **What to do with vague target ("I want something different")** | Accept it. Generate 3 *exploration* experiments instead of 3 *validation* experiments — informational interviews across 3 different fields, one short writing experiment to surface what you naturally notice, one shadow day. | Most users don't know what they're testing yet. Forcing premature specificity loses them. Exploration mode is a real mode of the skill. |

## Experiment Design Template

Every experiment the skill produces uses this exact structure:

```
### Experiment [N]: [Short verb-led title — e.g., "Run 5 user interviews"]

**Hypothesis:** [One sentence. A testable claim about you, the work, or the market.
Format: "If I [action], I will learn whether [specific thing about fit, demand, or
energy]."]

**Action:** [Specific, observable steps. Who you'll talk to, what you'll build, what
you'll publish. If a friend can't tell whether you did it, rewrite this section.]

**Timeline:** [Start date or week-of. First signal expected within 14 days.
Total duration ≤ 4 weeks.]

**Success signal:** [A specific behavior, energy pattern, or external response. Must
be observable. Examples: "I'm energized after 3 of 5 conversations." "2+ people DM
me asking how to do X." "I voluntarily pick this work up on a weekend."]

**Failure signal:** [The matching observable that says "no, drop this." Examples:
"I'm drained after 4 of 5 conversations." "Zero responses after publishing." "I keep
finding reasons to procrastinate the work itself."]

**If success → next step:** [The cheapest next experiment that escalates commitment.
Don't jump to "quit your job." Jump to the next-bigger test.]

**If failure → next step:** [A specific cheaper or different test. Failure is data,
not a verdict. The experiment ends, the exploration doesn't.]

**Effort:** [Total hours, honest estimate.]

**Risk to current role:** [None / Low / Medium. If Medium, name the specific risk.]
```

This template is what the skill outputs, three times, every time.
