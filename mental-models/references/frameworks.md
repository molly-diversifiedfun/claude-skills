# Facilitation Frameworks — All 12 Models

Each model includes: the unique question it answers, the step-by-step facilitation flow, the expected artifact, and estimated time. Follow these flows exactly — do not add or skip steps.

---

## 1. First Principles

**Answers:** "What's actually true underneath all the assumptions?"
**Steps:** 6 | **Time:** ~12 min
**Best for:** Innovation, convention-challenging, building from scratch

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — Define the target**
"What are you trying to solve or build? Give me one sentence."

Reflect it back to confirm scope. If it's too broad, ask them to narrow: "Which part of that is the actual bottleneck?"

**Step 2 — Surface assumptions**
"What are you taking for granted about this? List everything you assume is true — even the obvious stuff."

Push for 5-8 assumptions. If they stall at 3, prompt: "What about [cost / timeline / who's involved / how it's always been done]? Any assumptions hiding there?"

**Step 3 — Challenge each assumption**
Take each assumption one at a time:
"Is this actually true, or is it just how things are typically done? What evidence supports it?"

Mark each as: BEDROCK (definitely true), CONVENTION (assumed but not proven), or UNKNOWN (no evidence either way). Build a visible list.

**Step 4 — Identify fundamentals**
"Strip away the conventions and unknowns. What remains that's definitely true?"

Produce a numbered list of 2-4 bedrock truths. These are the foundation.

**Step 5 — Rebuild from fundamentals**
"Given only these truths, what solutions become possible that weren't visible before?"

Generate 2-3 novel approaches with the user. Push past the first idea: "That's one. What else becomes possible?"

**Step 6 — Feasibility check**
"Which of these is most realistic given your constraints? What's the first concrete step?"

**Artifact:** Assumption audit table (assumption / status / evidence) + bedrock truths list + reframed solution + first action step.

## 2. Pre-Mortem

**Answers:** "What specific risks am I blind to?"
**Steps:** 5 | **Time:** ~8 min
**Best for:** Plan stress-testing, launch prep, pre-commitment risk analysis

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — State the plan**
"Describe the plan you're about to execute in 2-3 sentences. What does success look like?"

**Step 2 — Project failure**
"Now imagine it's [appropriate timeframe — ask the user or infer from context] from now, and this has failed spectacularly. Not a small miss — a real failure. Don't hold back. What happened?"

Let them generate freely. Don't interrupt or evaluate.

**Step 3 — Extract discrete risks**
Organize their failure narrative into 3-5 discrete risk factors. Present the list back:

"I'm hearing these risks: [numbered list]. What else? What did we miss?"

Keep pushing with "What else?" until they're dry. Aim for 5 minimum.

**Step 4 — Score each risk**
For each risk, ask:
"How likely is this, 1-5? And how damaging if it happens, 1-5?"

Build a scored risk matrix:

| Risk | Likelihood (1-5) | Impact (1-5) | Score |
|------|------------------|--------------|-------|
| ... | ... | ... | L x I |

Sort by score descending.

**Step 5 — Mitigation actions**
For the top 2-3 risks (highest scores):
"What's one concrete thing you could do this week to reduce this risk?"

**Artifact:** Scored risk matrix table + mitigation action plan for top risks.

## 3. Second-Order Thinking

**Answers:** "What happens after what happens next?"
**Steps:** 4 | **Time:** ~8 min
**Best for:** Policy decisions, strategy, anything with downstream ripple effects

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — State the action**
"What's the action or decision you're considering? State it clearly."

**Step 2 — First-order effects**
"If you do this, what happens immediately? What are the direct, obvious consequences?"

List 3-5 first-order effects.

**Step 3 — Second and third-order effects**
Take each first-order effect one at a time:
"And then what happens? What does [first-order effect] cause next?"

Chain at least 2 levels deep for each. Build a visible consequence chain:

```
Action → [1st order] → [2nd order] → [3rd order]
```

Push for non-obvious effects: "Who else is affected by this that you haven't mentioned?"

**Step 4 — Decision check**
"Look at the full chain. Do the second and third-order effects change your decision? Are any of them dealbreakers?"

**Artifact:** Consequence chain diagram (text-based) showing action → 1st → 2nd → 3rd order effects, with the user's assessment of whether downstream effects change the decision.

## 4. Regret Minimization

**Answers:** "Will I regret not doing this in 20 years?"
**Steps:** 4 | **Time:** ~6 min
**Best for:** Irreversible life decisions, career changes, one-way doors

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — Frame the choice**
"What's the decision? State both options clearly — what you'd do, and what you'd do instead."

If more than two options, ask them to narrow: "Which two are you really torn between?"

**Step 2 — Project forward**
"You're 80 years old, looking back on your life. For each option: what would you specifically regret?"

Push for specificity: "What exactly would you wish you'd done differently?" Don't accept "I'd regret not trying" — ask: "Regret not trying what specifically?"

**Step 3 — Separate fear from values**
"Look at those regrets. Which ones come from genuine values — things that matter deeply to you? And which come from fear of discomfort, embarrassment, or short-term pain?"

Build two columns: VALUES-BASED REGRETS and FEAR-BASED REGRETS.

**Step 4 — Commit**
"Which path minimizes your deepest, values-based regret? And what's one thing you'd do this week to move toward it?"

**Artifact:** Regret analysis (option A regrets vs. option B regrets) + values vs. fear classification + decision + first action.

## 5. Opportunity Cost

**Answers:** "What am I invisibly giving up by choosing this?"
**Steps:** 4 | **Time:** ~7 min
**Best for:** Resource allocation, build vs. buy, trade-off clarity

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — State what you're choosing**
"What are you about to commit to? Be specific about the resources involved — time, money, energy, attention."

**Step 2 — Name the best alternative**
"What's the single best alternative you're NOT choosing? Not a vague 'something else' — name a specific thing you'd do with those same resources."

If they can't name one, help: "If you didn't do [this], what would you do with that [time/money/energy] instead?"

**Step 3 — Quantify the gap**
"What's the realistic upside of your current choice? And what's the realistic upside of the alternative? Try to put numbers or concrete outcomes on both."

Build a comparison:

| | Current choice | Best alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Investment (time/money/energy) | ... | ... |
| Realistic upside | ... | ... |
| Downside if it fails | ... | ... |

**Step 4 — Name the invisible price**
"Every yes is a no to something else. What are you invisibly paying by choosing this? Is the gap worth it?"

**Artifact:** Opportunity cost comparison table + the named invisible price + the user's verdict on whether the gap is worth it.

## 6. Eisenhower Matrix

**Answers:** "Am I spending time on urgent trivia instead of important work?"
**Steps:** 3 | **Time:** ~5 min
**Best for:** Overwhelm, prioritization, time management

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — Brain dump**
"List everything on your plate right now. Don't filter — just get it all out."

Push for completeness: "What about things you've been avoiding? Anything nagging at the back of your mind?"

**Step 2 — Classify each item**
Take each item and ask:
"Is this urgent (deadline or consequence if delayed)? Is this important (moves a meaningful goal forward)?"

Build the matrix:

| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | DO NOW: [items] | SCHEDULE: [items] |
| **Not Important** | DELEGATE/BATCH: [items] | ELIMINATE: [items] |

Challenge misclassifications: "Is [item] actually important, or does it just feel urgent?"

**Step 3 — Focus plan**
"Look at the SCHEDULE quadrant — Important but Not Urgent. These are your highest-leverage items that you're probably neglecting. When this week will you work on the top one?"

For DELEGATE/BATCH items: "Who could handle these, or could you batch them into one time block?"

For ELIMINATE items: "What happens if you just... don't do these?"

**Artifact:** Completed 4-quadrant Eisenhower matrix + focus plan for Important/Not Urgent items + delegation/elimination plan.

## 7. OODA Loop

**Answers:** "How do I act decisively when the situation keeps changing?"
**Steps:** 4 | **Time:** ~8 min
**Best for:** Crisis response, fast-moving environments, incomplete information

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — Observe**
"What are you seeing right now? Just the facts — what's actually happening, not your interpretation of it."

Push for observable facts over narratives: "That sounds like an interpretation. What's the raw data behind it?"

**Step 2 — Orient**
"Given your context — your goals, your constraints, your position — what does this information mean? What options does it open or close?"

This is the critical step. Help them connect observations to their specific situation: "How is this different from what you expected? What changed?"

**Step 3 — Decide**
"What's the minimum viable action? Not the perfect move — the smallest thing you can do right now that gives you more information or improves your position."

Push away from analysis paralysis: "You don't need the full plan. What's the next 24-48 hour move?"

**Step 4 — Act + Re-observe**
"Do it. Then: what new information will you look for to know if it worked? What signals will tell you to adjust?"

Set up the next observation cycle: "When will you check back? What would make you change course?"

**Artifact:** Observation summary (facts only) + orientation analysis + decided action + monitoring signals for the next loop.

## 8. Systems Thinking

**Answers:** "How do these parts interact and create feedback loops?"
**Steps:** 5 | **Time:** ~10 min
**Best for:** Interconnected problems, organizational dynamics, anything with feedback loops

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — Define the system boundary**
"What's the system we're looking at? Where do you draw the boundary — what's inside, what's outside?"

Help them scope: "If this were a diagram, what would be on it and what would be off the edges?"

**Step 2 — Map key components**
"What are the major parts of this system? Not everything — just the 4-6 elements that matter most."

List them. For each, ask: "Is this a resource, a process, a person/group, or a rule?"

**Step 3 — Identify connections and feedback loops**
Take the components and ask:
"How do these connect? When [A] changes, what happens to [B]?"

Specifically look for:
- **Reinforcing loops** (more A causes more B causes more A — growth spirals or death spirals)
- **Balancing loops** (more A causes less B causes more A — self-correcting)

Build a text-based map:

```
[Component A] --increases--> [Component B] --decreases--> [Component C] --increases--> [Component A]
(Balancing loop)
```

**Step 4 — Find leverage points**
"Where in this system would a small change produce the biggest effect? What's the bottleneck, the constraint, or the accelerator?"

**Step 5 — Intervention design**
"If you could change one thing in this system, what would it be? What feedback loop would that disrupt or amplify?"

**Artifact:** System map (components + connections + labeled feedback loops) + identified leverage points + proposed intervention.

## 9. Bayesian Updating

**Answers:** "How should this new evidence change my confidence?"
**Steps:** 4 | **Time:** ~7 min
**Best for:** Conflicting data, belief revision, forecast updating

### Facilitation Flow

**Important:** Use natural language throughout. No probability notation. Confidence on a 1-10 scale.

**Step 1 — State current belief**
"What do you currently believe about this? And on a scale of 1-10, how confident are you? 1 means 'barely a hunch,' 10 means 'I'd bet my house on it.'"

**Step 2 — Present the evidence**
"What's the new evidence or information that's making you reconsider?"

Clarify the evidence: "How reliable is the source? Is there any reason this evidence might be misleading?"

**Step 3 — Update confidence**
"Given this evidence, has your confidence changed? What number are you at now, and why?"

If they jump too far in either direction, probe: "That's a big shift. Is this evidence really strong enough to move you that much? What would have to be true for this evidence to be misleading?"

If they don't move at all, probe: "If this evidence doesn't change your confidence even a little, what evidence would?"

Build the update:

```
Belief: [statement]
Before evidence: [X]/10
New evidence: [description]
After evidence: [Y]/10
Reasoning: [why the shift]
```

**Step 4 — Calibrate**
"What evidence would move you significantly in either direction? What would make you a 9? What would drop you to a 3?"

This identifies what information they should actively seek.

**Artifact:** Belief statement + confidence update (before/after with reasoning) + evidence that would change their mind in either direction.

## 10. Steel-Manning

**Answers:** "What's the strongest version of the argument I disagree with?"
**Steps:** 5 | **Time:** ~10 min
**Best for:** Debates, bias-checking, position refinement

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — State your position**
"What's your current view on this? State it clearly."

**Step 2 — Identify the opposition**
"Who disagrees with you, and what's their position? Not a strawman — what do thoughtful, informed people on the other side actually say?"

If they caricature the opposition: "That's the weak version. What would the smartest person who disagrees say?"

**Step 3 — Build the strongest opposing case**
"Now argue their side as persuasively as you can. What evidence supports them? What are they right about?"

This is the core exercise. Push hard: "You're being too generous to your own side. What's the single strongest point against your position?"

**Step 4 — Find the tension point**
"Where does their strongest point directly challenge your weakest assumption? What's the specific tension?"

**Step 5 — Refine or revise**
"Given this, do you want to: modify your position, add a caveat or condition, or directly address the vulnerability?"

All three are valid outcomes. The goal is a more robust position, not necessarily a changed one.

**Artifact:** Original position + steel-manned opposition (the strongest version) + identified tension point + refined position statement.

## 11. 5 Whys

**Answers:** "What's the root cause underneath the symptoms?"
**Steps:** 5 | **Time:** ~7 min
**Best for:** Recurring failures, diagnostics, problems that keep coming back

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — State the symptom**
"What's the problem as you see it right now? Describe what's actually happening."

Anchor on observable behavior, not interpretations: "What specifically are you seeing or experiencing?"

**Step 2 — Why #1**
"Why is that happening?"

If the answer is vague, probe: "Can you be more specific? What's the mechanism — how does [cause] lead to [symptom]?"

**Step 3 — Why #2**
"And why is [their answer to Why #1] happening?"

Watch for logic jumps. If they skip a level: "Hold on — how does [A] lead to [B]? Is there a step in between?"

**Step 4 — Why #3-4**
Continue with "Why?" for 1-2 more rounds. At each level:
- Check for evidence: "How do you know this is the cause? What would you see if it weren't?"
- Watch for blame loops: If they land on "because [person] is bad at their job," redirect: "What about the system makes that possible? Why does that failure mode exist?"

Build the visible causal chain:

```
Symptom: [original problem]
  Why? → [cause 1]
    Why? → [cause 2]
      Why? → [cause 3]
        Why? → [cause 4 / root cause]
```

**Step 5 — Root cause and intervention**
"We've reached a cause you can actually act on. What would change if you addressed [root cause] directly? What's the intervention?"

Test the chain backwards: "If you fixed [root cause], would [symptom] go away? Or is there another branch?"

**Artifact:** 4-5 level causal chain (visually formatted) + identified root cause + intervention plan.

## 12. Decision Matrix

**Answers:** "How do these 3+ options compare across my criteria?"
**Steps:** 5 | **Time:** ~10 min
**Best for:** Multiple competing options, structured comparison, group decisions

### Facilitation Flow

**Step 1 — List options**
"What are your options? List them all — we'll narrow if needed."

If more than 6: "That's a lot. Can we cut any that you know aren't serious contenders?" Aim for 3-5.

**Step 2 — Define criteria**
"What matters to you in this decision? Name 3-5 criteria you'd use to judge these options."

If they struggle, prompt by category: "Think about: cost, speed, risk, alignment with goals, effort required, reversibility."

**Step 3 — Weight criteria**
"Not all criteria are equal. Rate each one: is it a MUST-HAVE (deal-breaker if missing) or NICE-TO-HAVE?"

For nice-to-haves, assign relative weight: "Of the nice-to-haves, which matters most? Give each a weight from 1-3."

Must-haves get a weight of 5 (they're binary — pass or fail).

**Step 4 — Score each option**
Build the matrix. For each option on each criterion:
"Score [option] on [criterion], 1-5. 1 is terrible, 5 is excellent."

| Criterion | Weight | Option A | Option B | Option C |
|-----------|--------|----------|----------|----------|
| [criterion 1] | [wt] | [score] | [score] | [score] |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| **Weighted Total** | | **[sum]** | **[sum]** | **[sum]** |

Calculate weighted totals: score x weight for each cell, sum the column.

**Step 5 — Sensitivity check**
"[Option with highest score] wins on the numbers. But let's stress-test: if you changed your top-weighted criterion, would the winner change?"

Also: "Does the winner feel right? If your gut says no, what criterion is missing from the matrix?"

If gut and matrix disagree, that's valuable data — there's an unspoken criterion.

**Artifact:** Completed weighted decision matrix + winner + sensitivity check result + any unspoken criteria surfaced.
