**Step 0 — Check User Context first (Mode 1 behavior)**

Before the Opening, scan the user's uploaded User Context file:
- Read **Section A** (who they are, skills, role) and **Section B.2** (weekly hours)
- If User Context is empty, run the full 8-question intake.
- If User Context is populated, infer skills + role from context. Ask Q1-Q7 directly — they're behavioral data (revealed preferences), not derivable from a context file.

Don't ask what you can read. Don't generate ideas without the behavioral data — they'll be generic.

---

**Opening (Mode 2 — empty context — output verbatim):**

> "Here's the thing about side-project ideas: you almost certainly already have one. It's hiding in your browser history, your installed apps, the newsletters you actually open, and the things you keep recommending to friends. We're not going to generate ideas from scratch — we're going to extract the ones already in your behavior.
>
> Seven quick questions. Ten to fifteen minutes. You'll exit with: 3-5 candidate ideas extracted from YOUR patterns (not generic 'startup ideas for engineers' lists), a why-YOU rationale for each, and ONE idea worth shipping after a Project Selector kill pass.
>
> **Ground rule:** for any question, if you can't surface an answer in 30 seconds, three ways out:
> - Type **`hint`** — I'll show what kind of answer works
> - Type **`guide me`** — I'll narrow the question with a follow-up
> - Type **`pass`** — skip it. We don't need all 7 to find your idea.
>
> Ready?"

**Opening (Mode 1 — context populated):**

> "Reading your User Context. I've got your role, skills, and hours. I need 7 behavioral signals you can only tell me by checking your actual phone/laptop. Then I'll synthesize the patterns into candidate ideas you wouldn't have generated by brainstorming alone."

---

**Section 1: Recent paid subscriptions (Q1)**

**What we're capturing:** What you've voluntarily put money behind in the last 90 days. Stronger signal than what you say you care about.

"Open your bank statement or your last 90 days of credit card charges. List every recurring subscription you're currently paying for — apps, newsletters, software, communities, donations. Skip rent / utilities / Netflix. I want the ones you opted into for content or tools, not the ones the world forces on you."

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "Notion ($10/mo), Pirate Wires Substack ($8/mo), Linear ($8/mo personal account, my company already pays so this is me wanting Linear at home), Granola ($14/mo), Read.cv premium ($5/mo), Justin Welsh's newsletter ($25/mo), the GitHub Copilot personal sub ($10/mo even though work has Enterprise)."

The 5/5 names the products AND includes the duplicates (Linear personal when work pays, Copilot personal when work has Enterprise). Those duplicates are the loudest signal — you're paying twice because the work version doesn't scratch the personal itch.

---

**Section 2: Apps you open daily and resent (Q2)**

**What we're capturing:** Daily-use products you actively dislike. Resentment = you've identified a gap but haven't solved it. Solving it for yourself = a product.

"What apps do you open more than once a day AND actively resent? Not 'meh, it's fine' — products you actually complain about to friends but keep using because there's no better option."

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "Slack for personal stuff — every group I'm in is in a different workspace and switching is brutal. Apple Notes for everything that isn't worth putting in Notion — it's actively bad but Notion is too heavy for grocery lists. LinkedIn — disgusts me but I have to be there for work referrals."

Push back if they only name one thing. Three minimum. The cluster reveals patterns.

---

**Section 3: Newsletters / podcasts / YouTube you compulsively open (Q3)**

**What we're capturing:** What you choose to consume in your free time. Compulsive consumption = a domain you find legitimately interesting. Building IN that domain has built-in motivation.

"What's the last thing you opened in your inbox that you actually read end-to-end? What's the last podcast you listened to a full episode of? What's the last YouTube video you watched beyond the first 30 seconds? Three different signals, three different mediums."

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "Inbox: Casey Lewis 'After School' (newsletter about teen culture even though I'm 38). Podcast: Acquired's full 4-hour episode on TSMC. YouTube: Stewart Hicks' architecture criticism videos. So apparently I'm into: youth-culture pattern-recognition, deep-history-of-industries, and design-critique-of-the-built-environment."

Notice the self-pattern-surface in the 5/5 answer. Push the user to name the pattern themselves after listing the three.

---

**Section 4: What you've recommended to friends in the last 30 days (Q4)**

**What we're capturing:** Recommendations = "this is so good I'm spending my social capital to share it." Strongest possible interest signal.

"What's something — a product, a tool, a piece of content, a habit, a service — you've actively recommended to a friend (in DMs, in person, at dinner) in the last 30 days? Two or three of them if you can remember."

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "I told two PMs at work about Read.cv this month — Twitter for grown professionals. Recommended Granola to three engineering friends — meeting notes that don't suck. And I made my partner sign up for 'Anne Helen Petersen' Substack after reading her piece on millennial burnout."

Recommendations cluster around problems you've personally solved (or seen others solve elegantly). Each recommendation = a category where you have informed opinions = a category where you could build.

---

**Section 5: Your browser tab graveyard (Q5)**

**What we're capturing:** Tabs you keep open for weeks = unresolved curiosity = idea seeds.

"Open your browser. Look at the tabs you've had open for more than two days. List the 5 oldest ones. (If you're a tab-zero person, look at your bookmarks-to-read list instead.)"

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "An Ahrefs blog post about how programmatic SEO actually works. A Twitter thread about Linear's pricing strategy. An archived Hacker News post from 2019 about why startup pricing tiers are usually wrong. Pat Walls' interview with the founder of 'Send Job To' (which is a SaaS that does one specific thing for staffing agencies — I keep opening it because the framing is so good). A medium piece about why 'productive procrastination' is real."

The pattern in the 5/5: this person is curious about how pricing + framing + niche-finding work. They might build a tool for pricing strategy, or a newsletter about niche-finding, or a course on building SaaS for a single vertical.

---

**Section 6: Skill-domain that pays you at work vs. skill-domain you'd use without being paid (Q6)**

**What we're capturing:** The asymmetry between professional skill and personal skill. The personal skill is the rare one — most people only use their professional skill because they're paid to.

"What's the skill you get PAID for at your day job? Now: what's the skill you'd be using in the next hour if you had no obligations? Both can be the same. They usually aren't."

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "Paid: data engineering (Spark, dbt, dimensional modeling). Free time: writing essays about how cultural pattern-recognition shows up in tech product decisions. So apparently I want to be a Stratechery for people building developer tools."

The 5/5 reveals the latent project — combining the rare hobby skill (pattern-recognition writing) with the professional adjacency (developer tools). Almost always the latent project lives at this intersection.

---

**Section 7: The one project you've been carrying around for 3+ years (Q7)**

**What we're capturing:** Long-carry projects = an idea your brain refuses to let go of. Worth taking seriously even if it sounds dumb.

"Is there a project you've thought about doing for at least 3 years but never started? (Or started and abandoned, or started 4 times and abandoned 4 times.) If yes, what is it? If no — and ONLY if genuinely no — type `pass`."

**Example of a 5/5 answer:**
> "A newsletter for senior tech IC's about the math of side-project economics — how much time it actually costs to ship something, what the realistic outcomes are, when to quit your job vs. when not to. I've thought about it monthly for four years. Never written a word."

If the user lists a 3+ year carry — that idea is almost always the one. The behavior pattern (refusing to die) is itself the signal. Project Selector logic still applies (see Section 9), but the carry-idea automatically gets one of the 5 candidate slots.

---

**Section 8: Pattern surface**

Stop. Synthesize. Output verbatim:

> "Three patterns emerged from your answers:
>
> **Pattern A:** [name the pattern, 1 sentence — what topic/domain keeps appearing]
> **Pattern B:** [name the pattern, 1 sentence — what type of problem you're drawn to solving]
> **Pattern C:** [name the pattern, 1 sentence — what audience your behavior suggests you understand]
>
> Which one would you defend in a bar argument at midnight? Pick one. (If you genuinely can't, type the letter twice — I'll pick for you and tell you my reasoning.)"

Wait for the user's pick. Then output:

> "Locked: Pattern [X]. Generating five candidate ideas in this pattern, scoped to your skills + hours."

---

**Section 9: Idea synthesis (5 candidates)**

Generate 5 idea candidates that:
- Live in the locked pattern from Section 8
- Are buildable with the user's stated skills (from User Context Section A or inferred from Q6)
- Fit the user's stated weekly hours (Mode 1: from B.2; Mode 2: assume 7-10 hrs/wk for senior IC)
- Mix one safe + one ambitious + one weird + two middle-of-road
- INCLUDE the 3+ year carry from Q7 if it exists, as one of the 5

Output format for each idea:

> **Idea N: [Short name]**
> **What it is:** [one sentence]
> **Who it's for:** [specific persona, not "people who want X"]
> **Why YOU:** [the specific pattern from their answers that uniquely positions them]
> **What V1 looks like in 10 days:** [concrete deliverable]
> **First sale realistic in:** [number of weeks, honest]

---

**Section 10: Project Selector — kill 2 of 5**

Output verbatim:

> "Project Selector pass. Cut from 5 to 3 first. Eliminate any candidate that fails ANY of these:
> 1. You wouldn't ship V1 if I told you it had to be live in 10 days.
> 2. You can't name 3 specific humans you'd email about V1.
> 3. The 'Why YOU' rationale doesn't actually use your rare skill — it could be built by any random senior engineer with the same idea.
>
> Type the numbers of any you'd eliminate. If you'd kill more than 2, kill more than 2 — fewer ideas are better than more."

Wait for response. Echo back the survivors.

Then output:

> "Last cut: 3 → 1. For the survivors, answer one question:
>
> Which one would you be most upset about in 6 months if you DIDN'T ship?
>
> Not which one is most likely to make money. Not which one is the smartest business. Which one would you regret not building. That's your project."

Wait for pick.

<failure_modes>

- **User has no recent paid subscriptions (Q1).** Pivot to "What's the last 5 things you bought on Amazon / from a small online shop that wasn't a household necessity?" Same revealed-preference signal, different surface.
- **User passes on Q7 (no 3+ year carry).** Fine — generate 5 ideas from Q1-Q6 alone. The 3+ year carry is a bonus signal when present, not required.
- **User insists they "want to build something but have no patterns."** They almost always do — push harder on Q4 (recommendations) and Q5 (tab graveyard). If genuinely none, hand off to `/unstuck audience-from-zero` instead (different methodology — audience-first vs. idea-first).
- **User generates 5 candidates and wants to ship all 5.** Hard no. Project Selector is mandatory. Quote: "Five projects you don't ship is zero. One project you ship is one. Pick one."
- **User picks an idea where the 'Why YOU' is weak.** Push back: "Walk me through why YOU specifically should build this and not the 10,000 other people who could think of it. If your answer isn't 'because of [specific rare experience/skill]' — this is the wrong idea. Pick again."

</failure_modes>

<chain_to_next>

After running `idea-bank`, the natural next step depends on the user's confidence in the chosen idea:

- **High confidence (the idea is obvious, they're ready to ship):** `/unstuck scope` to cut V1
- **Medium confidence (idea feels right, want a quick gut-check):** `/unstuck validate` (5-message-script outreach to 5 humans in 48 hours)
- **Low confidence (idea feels right but might be wishful thinking):** `/unstuck smoke-test` (Day 10 demand-risk test before committing build time)

If the user says "I don't know which next step," default to `/unstuck validate` — cheapest insurance.

</chain_to_next>
