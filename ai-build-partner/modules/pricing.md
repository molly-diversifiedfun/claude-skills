>

> 2. **Find the 3 alternatives** (Professional / Course / DIY) with real prices

**Step 1 — Lock the outcome**

Ask:

> "Your product delivers what outcome? Be specific — 'save time' is a vibe; 'save 4 hours every Sunday on invoicing' is a sale. Give me the specific version."

If vague → push back ("'help people make money' isn't priceable — money on what, by when, at what effort level"). Force specificity.

Lock the outcome in one sentence.

---

**Step 2 — Map the 3 alternatives**

Pull from User Context Section B + the locked outcome. Research/estimate 3 alternative paths the buyer's customer would take to solve this problem TODAY:

**Alternative 1 — Professional help / done-for-you:**

> "What would it cost your audience to hire someone (coach, consultant, agency, VA, designer) to deliver this outcome for them? Give me realistic LOW / MID / HIGH market prices.
>
> Example for 'save 4 hrs/week on invoicing': hire a designer to create custom invoices = $500-1,500. Hire a freelance bookkeeper to run invoicing = $200/mo retainer."

**Alternative 2 — Online course / cohort / community:**

> "What's the going rate for a course covering this outcome? Name 2-3 real comparable products if you can.
>
> Example: Skillshare invoice course = $40/mo or $200/yr. Lenny's premium = $300/yr. Make Time = $250 one-time."

**Alternative 3 — DIY (time × hourly rate):**

> "How many hours would your buyer realistically need to solve this themselves? Multiply by their plausible billed hourly rate. Apply Cargill (1985) — people underestimate this by ~2x.
>
> Example: 8 hrs research + 8 hrs build = 16 hrs (Cargill = 32 hrs) × $50/hr = $1,600 in opportunity cost."

Output the 3 alternatives with real numbers.

---

**Step 3 — Pick the anchor (the WORST realistic alternative, not the cheapest)**

> "Picking the cheapest alternative underprices you. Picking the worst realistic alternative is honest about what people ACTUALLY do today. Most people don't take the Skillshare course — they DIY for 32 hours and lose a Saturday. The DIY anchor is usually the strongest.
>
> Ariely (2008): anchor at the highest defensible number. Your price feels reasonable relative to the anchor, not in absolute terms."

Pick the highest realistic anchor from Step 2.

> "Your anchor = $[paste]."

---

**Step 4 — Set Value % × Cost %**

For the locked anchor of $[ANCHOR]:

| Value % delivered | Cost % charged | Resulting price |
|---|---|---|
| 80% of value | at 80% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.64] |
| 80% of value | at 50% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.40] |
| 80% of value | at 20% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.16] |
| 50% of value | at 80% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.40] |
| 50% of value | at 50% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.25] |
| 50% of value | at 20% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.10] |
| 20% of value | at 50% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.10] |
| 20% of value | at 20% of cost | $[ANCHOR × 0.04] |

Ask: "What % of the value does your product deliver vs. the anchor? What % of the cost are you willing to charge?"

Compute the price target.

> "Your price target = $___ (based on [X%] value × [Y%] cost of $[ANCHOR])."

---

**Step 5 — Cross-check against price-point signal table**

| Price | Signal | Typical format | Customer type |
|---|---|---|---|
| **$17** | Impulse, "I'm curious" | Guide, swipe file, small template | Just exploring |
| **$27** | Legitimate, some real value | Mini-course, template kit | Serious but budget-conscious |
| **$47** | Real product, investment-level | Template kit, mini-course, guide | Knows they need help |
| **$97** | Premium, "I need expert help" | Full mini-course, template system | Ready to invest |
| **$197+** | Luxury, high specialization | Intensive course, coaching | Committed buyer |

Cross-check Step 4 price against the buyer's intended customer type:

- If price = $47 but customer type is "luxury committed buyer" → the price under-positions you. Raise.
- If price = $197 but customer type is "just exploring" → wrong audience-fit, not wrong price.

Push back if there's a mismatch.

---

**Step 6 — Apply Molly's rule + lock the price**

> "Molly's rule: when in doubt, charge more. You can always discount. You can't easily raise. If you're torn between $47 and $67, pick $67. The buyer who pays $67 is more committed; refund rate is lower; support volume is lower."

Lock the final price.

> "Your V1 price = $___."
