**Step 1 — Lock the urgency mechanism for Email 5 (FIRST, not last)**

Most launches die because Email 5 has fake urgency. Lock the real one before drafting anything.

Ask:

> "Email 5 needs ONE real urgency mechanism. Pick:
> - **Price actually going up** (raise on Day 8 — true price, not 'launch price' theater)
> - **Bonus actually expiring** (the bonus disappears, not 'limited spots' if there's no real cap)
> - **Cohort/cap actually closing** (only if the seat count is real)
> - **Last email** ('after today, I'm moving on to V2') — only if true
>
> Which one's actually true for your launch? If none, we're going to design one — or Email 5 won't ship."

If buyer picks something that isn't actually true, push back:

> "If 'limited spots' isn't actually capped, customers smell it. Real scarcity beats fake every time (Cialdini, 1984). Pick a real one or design one — e.g., 'price goes to $X on [date]' is a real urgency you can actually enforce."

Lock the mechanism. Everything downstream anchors to it.

---

**Step 2 — Email 1: The Story (Day 1)**

**Purpose:** Create connection. Why did you build this?

Subject A + Subject B variants. Use the slippery-slide opener (Sugarman 1984) — first sentence delivers to the second; never let them stop reading.

Ask:

> "Email 1 opens with a specific moment. Not 'I'm excited to announce' — a date, a place, what you were doing, what you were feeling. Tell me the SPECIFIC moment you decided to build this. (One sentence is fine. I'll expand it.)"

If the buyer goes abstract ("I noticed people kept asking…"), push:

> "That's the theme. I need the moment. Was it a specific Tuesday afternoon? A specific DM? A specific drive home from work? Specific moment, please."

Draft Email 1 (≤ 280 words):

- Specific opening moment (the slippery-slide hook)
- Bridge: "That's when I realized…"
- One-sentence product introduction
- SOFT CTA (no hard sell — give them a way to learn more)

---

**Step 3 — Email 2: What's Inside (Day 2)**

**Purpose:** Show value. Features → benefits.

Pull V1 features from User Context D.2. Reuse hero subhead from D.8 in the first line.

Format:

```
Yesterday I told you the story behind [PRODUCT].
Today, what's actually inside.

[Feature 1]: [Benefit]
[Feature 2]: [Benefit]
[Feature 3]: [Benefit]
[Feature 4]: [Benefit]
[Feature 5]: [Benefit]

[BRIDGE: why these features solve the original problem from Email 1.]

[STRONG CTA — direct link to checkout]
```

≤ 250 words. ONE CTA.

---

**Step 4 — Email 3: The Proof (Day 4)**

**Purpose:** Build credibility.

Two paths:

**Path A — You have testimonials:**

Ask which 3, what each proves. Use specifically-named customers (first name + title/context). The story shape: SPECIFIC outcome + the quote that lands it.

**Path B — You don't have testimonials yet:**

Don't fake them. Ever. Use the substitute structure:

> "Here's what happened when *I* started using this myself. [Specific outcome on your own life.] Result: [specific number / artifact / change]."

Update Email 3 to real customer stories after your first 3 customer wins land.

Either path: ≤ 230 words, ONE CTA, no fabricated quotes.

---

**Step 5 — Email 4: The Objections (Day 6)**

**Purpose:** Answer doubts before they kill the sale.

Pull real objections from User Context D.1 (validation conversation themes). No strawmen.

Format:

```
You might be wondering…

"[Real objection 1]"
[Answer in 1-2 sentences. Direct. No deflecting.]

"[Real objection 2]"
[Answer]

"[Real objection 3]"
[Answer]

[CLOSING: remove friction. Make it easy to say yes.]
[CTA]
```

Three objections max. ≤ 250 words. ONE CTA.

Goldsmith (2015) objection-sequencing: lead with the easiest objection to dismantle, end with the hardest. Builds momentum before the harder ask.

---

**Step 6 — Email 5: Last Chance (Day 7) + afternoon reminder**

**Purpose:** Genuine urgency. The locked mechanism from Step 1.

≤ 220 words for morning send. ≤ 80 words for afternoon reminder (4 PM).

Subject:

- Morning: question or specific time ("Closing at midnight" not "Last call!!!")
- Afternoon reminder: short urgency anchor ("6 hours left")

Structure:

```
This is the last email I'm sending about [PRODUCT].

[GENUINE URGENCY: the mechanism from Step 1, stated specifically with date/time.]

[REMINDER: what they get, why it matters. 2-3 lines max.]

[FINAL CTA]
```

Push back if the buyer wants to send a 6th email:

> "Day 7 was the last. The whole point of 'this is the last email' is that it's true. If you send a 6th, you teach the list that your urgency isn't real, and Email 5 of your *next* launch lands flat."

If any fails → fix before shipping.
