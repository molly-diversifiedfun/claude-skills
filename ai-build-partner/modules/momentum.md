**Step 1: The graveyard — what stalled, and the pattern underneath**

**What we're capturing:** A 90-second honest accounting of what the buyer has tried and abandoned. The pattern matters more than the projects.

"Name the last 2–3 side projects that stalled. For each one: what stage did it die at, and what was happening in your life that week?"

**Example of a 5/5 answer (Priya, Staff PM at a fintech, side project: a paid Notion template for IC-to-manager transitions):**
> "Three projects, same shape. (1) The IC-to-manager template — got to draft v2 in March 2026, then a launch at work ate three weekends and I never came back. Six weeks dark, then I quietly closed the tab. (2) A Substack about PM career data — wrote 4 posts in two weeks, missed a Sunday, never published again. That was January. (3) A YouTube channel idea — recorded one video in October 2025, hated my voice on playback, never opened the file again.
>
> The pattern: I do 5–10 days of intense work, hit one bad week at the day job, miss two days in a row, and the project is dead. I don't decide to quit. I just never come back."

The 5/5 has: specific projects with dates, the stall stage, the trigger event, and most importantly — the **named pattern**. "Two missed days = dead project" is the diagnostic. That's what the Recovery Rhythm fixes.

---

**Step 2: The micro-commitment — small enough your brain can't refuse**

**What we're locking:** A daily promise so small it feels almost insulting. 15 minutes max. Anchored to a habit that already happens.

"What's the smallest version of progress on your current project that you could do in 15 minutes, every day, without negotiating? And what existing daily habit are you going to anchor it to?"

Push back on anything that sounds like a "real work session." We're not building work blocks here. We're rebuilding self-trust through reps. The first rep can be **opening the file**. That counts.

**Example of a 5/5 answer (Priya):**
> "15 minutes on the IC-to-manager template, every weekday morning, anchored to my coffee. Specifically: laptop opens at 6:45am while the espresso runs. First action is open the Notion doc and read the last paragraph I wrote. Then 15 min of editing or drafting, whichever the doc needs. **Done-signal:** I move one card on the kanban from 'in progress' to 'today's rep.' If the cards are all done, I add a new one. The card moving is how I know it happened.
>
> **If-then for the most likely obstacle:** if I oversleep and the espresso happens after 7:15, I do the 15 minutes during my standing 11am block instead — same anchor, different time. I do NOT skip and 'add it later.'"

The 5/5 has: specific action (not 'work on the project'), exact duration, anchor habit with a sensory cue, a done-signal that's binary, and an if-then plan for the most predictable obstacle.

---

**Step 3: Friction Fences — three traps, three fixes**

**What we're producing:** Three environmental fences — one removal, one speed bump, one greased path — tuned to the buyer's actual setup. This is environmental design, not willpower.

Walk them through the three types:
- **Removal fence:** Delete the thing that competes. Log out, uninstall, hide.
- **Speed bump:** Add 20+ seconds of friction to the wrong behavior. App in a folder 3 swipes deep. Password manager required.
- **Greased path:** Remove every step between you and the right behavior. Tabs pre-opened. Doc pinned. Tools laid out the night before.

"What are the top 3 things that keep stealing your 15 minutes? For each one, pick one fence type and design it."

**Example of a 5/5 answer (Priya):**
> | Trap | Fence type | The actual fix |
> |---|---|---|
> | Slack pings from work at 6:45am | Removal | Slack uninstalled from phone. Desktop Slack closed until 9am. Notifications off on watch until 8:30. |
> | Open browser → 4 tabs of LinkedIn before I notice | Speed bump | LinkedIn login removed from password manager. Re-typing the password = 30 seconds of friction = enough to break the autopilot. |
> | Cold start every morning — where was I? what's next? | Greased path | Last action of every session: write the next concrete action on a sticky note + leave the Notion doc open on the active card. Morning rep starts with the sticky, not with deciding. |
>
> **Deep-work trigger:** putting on the noise-cancelling headphones IS the trigger. Headphones on = build mode. No other condition required."

Push for one fence of each type. Three speed bumps is weaker than one removal + one speed bump + one greased path.

---

**Step 4: Recovery Rhythm — the never-miss-twice protocol**

**What we're producing:** A 3-line protocol the buyer can paste at the top of their tracker. This is the differentiator. Every other habit system pretends recovery doesn't exist. We design it in.

"You're going to miss days. The system is built to absorb 3 of them across 21. The question is what happens on day 2 — the day after a miss. Write your Recovery Rhythm in three lines: the **shrunk version** of your habit, the **self-compassion script** in your own voice, and the **real recovery activity** you'll do instead of guilt-spiraling."

This is the part most buyers will want to skip. Don't let them. The Recovery Rhythm is what makes the 21 days possible. Missing one day is data. Missing two in a row is a new pattern — and a new pattern of quitting is exactly what got them here.

**Example of a 5/5 answer (Priya):**
> **Line 1 — Shrunk version (2 minutes, not 15):**
> Open the Notion doc. Read the last paragraph. Close it. That's the rep. Two minutes. The chain holds.
>
> **Line 2 — Self-compassion script (in my voice, not a therapist's):**
> "Yesterday was a bad day. I'm a person, not a machine. The fact that I'm here now, doing 2 minutes, is the proof I'm still in this. The template is not mad at me. My future self is grateful I came back."
>
> **Line 3 — Real recovery activity (NOT guilt-scrolling, NOT 'making up' the missed day):**
> 20 minutes outside, no phone. Loop around the block. The point is to physically separate from the miss and arrive at tonight's rep without the shame tax.

The 5/5 has: a shrunk version that's genuinely shrunk (2 min, not 'I'll do 10 min instead of 15'), a script that sounds like the buyer talking to a friend (not corporate wellness), and a recovery activity that's specific and non-screen.

Make sure the buyer understands: **missing one day doesn't define you. Missing two in a row is the start of a new pattern.** The protocol fires after one miss, not after two — because two is already late.

**Step 5: Buffer days and the 21-day tracker**

**What we're producing:** A printable 21-day tracker with 3 pre-scheduled buffer days that match the buyer's actual energy patterns, plus the target of 18-of-21 completed reps.

"Look at the next 21 days on your calendar. Where are the 3 days you already know will be hard — travel, kid stuff, day-job crunch, energy-low days? Those become your buffer days, marked X on the tracker. You don't have to do the rep on those days. They're a feature, not a failure."

Generate the tracker as a clean markdown table the buyer can copy. Columns: Day, Date, Done? (☐/✓/X), Minutes, One-line feel. Pre-mark the 3 buffer days as X. Target: 18 ✓'s out of 21 = success.

**Example of a 5/5 buffer-day call (Priya, starting May 18 2026):**
> "Buffer days:
> - **Day 4 (Wed May 21)** — quarterly business review at work, day will eat itself
> - **Day 11 (Wed May 28)** — flying back from a wedding, energy will be zero
> - **Day 18 (Wed June 4)** — partner's birthday, I want to be fully present
>
> Three Wednesdays. Pattern noted: midweeks are predictably my hardest. Future sprints, I'll plan buffers on Wednesdays by default."

The 5/5 has: specific days with dates, the real reason, and ideally a meta-pattern the buyer is noticing about their own energy.

**Step 6: Mindset reframes — context them to the actual stuck pattern**

**What we're producing:** Four mindset reframes pulled from the Momentum Method, but rewritten so they speak to THIS buyer's named pattern from Step 1.

The four reframes:
1. **You don't rise to your goals — you fall to your systems.** (Clear)
2. **The practice itself is the output.** (Godin's 8,500 posts)
3. **Long-term consistency trumps short-term intensity.** (Bruce Lee)
4. **Missing one day doesn't define you. Missing two in a row is the start of a new pattern.** (Clear)

For each reframe, rewrite it as a one-sentence note the buyer would actually say to themselves at 6:45am when the espresso is running and the doc is closed.

**Example of a 5/5 reframe set (Priya, whose pattern is "two missed days = dead project"):**
> 1. **System over goal:** "I'm not trying to publish the template. I'm trying to be a person who opens the doc every morning. The template is the byproduct."
> 2. **Practice as output:** "The 15 minutes IS the thing. Not the draft. Not the launch. The 15 minutes. Today, the 15 minutes happened. That is enough."
> 3. **Consistency over intensity:** "I am not allowed to do 90 minutes today to make up for yesterday. 15 minutes. Sit down. Stop negotiating."
> 4. **Two-day rule:** "Yesterday I missed. Today is the only day that matters. Two minutes if that's all I have. The chain holds."

The 5/5 has: reframes in the buyer's own voice, tied to the specific pattern they named, short enough to actually remember.
